# 金毛狗

**Date:** 2025-12-23 01:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷

起初听人说起[**印度**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/印度)神庙，脑海中浮现出雄伟庙宇与金身神像的画面，如同我曾多次拜访的杭州灵隐寺，庄严宏大，香烟氤氲，令人心生虔敬，但[**新泽西州**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/新泽西州)的BAPS斯瓦米纳拉扬寺庙（BAPS Shri Swaminarayan Mandir）却是另一番景象。

这座由大理石建成的建筑通体雪白，线条流利，既巍峨庄严，又纯净空灵，外观更类似欧洲的大教堂，被誉为大理石奇观。

一个夏日，我们驱车来到此处。一下车，映入眼帘的是粉色围墙和牌楼，随即是一个碧水微漾的水池，池里几束小喷泉轻轻地溅起水花。环绕水池的是绿植和红砖小径，悠扬的梵音在天地间回荡，营造出一种静心的氛围。在欢迎中心，有工作人员提醒游客注意服装，要求上衣有袖、裤裙过膝。

通向主庙的广场两侧是粉色的回廊，廊柱上雕刻着手持不同乐器的天人乐伎，形态各异，神态生动。主庙尖顶高耸入云，在阳光下显得洁白耀眼，气势恢宏，宛如一座石之殿堂。

进入殿堂需脱鞋。我脱下凉鞋，赤脚踏上大理石地面，冰凉的触感让我一时不太习惯。管理人员微笑着解释：赤足更能感受寺庙的神圣与能量。我试着走了几步，最终还是折返回车上穿了袜子，这一来一回耗去了半个多小时。

主庙内禁止拍照，这份限制却让心境更为沉静。安静的氛围、柔和的光影与精美的雕刻完美融合，创造出一种超脱尘世的体验。除了神像，廊柱与石墙上的精致浮雕也引人入胜。

这些浮雕工艺精湛，场面繁复，人物姿态自然传神，无声地讲述着过往圣贤的故事。头顶上，天花板的莲花式雕刻层层盛开，而中央的穹顶则最为壮观，那层层交叠的几何结构，仿佛是宇宙秩序在空中凝结。

这座现代宏伟建筑，由数千块在印度预制雕刻的大理石，运至美国后组装而成。主殿与副殿的神像，共同构筑了斯瓦米纳拉扬（Swaminarayan）教派丰富的神圣世界，而至高无上的主神便是斯瓦米纳拉扬。这位圣者在少年时期便展开长达七年、超过一万两千公里的苦行与朝圣之旅，最终在印度西部奠定了这一宗教传统。

这段历史不禁让我联想到赫尔曼·黑塞笔下的「悉达多」，想到释迦牟尼佛的故事。虽然斯瓦米纳拉扬与释迦牟尼相隔两千多年，一个奠立佛教，一个继承并改革了印度教毗湿奴派的传统，他们都在青少年时期毅然舍弃世俗生活，走上修行之路，最终成为大规模宗教的奠基者。他们都强调戒律与道德，提倡众生平等。他们的生命轨迹，跨越时空，散发着相似的皎皎光辉。

尽管这座雪白的大理石殿堂给人以欧洲教堂的典雅之感，但亚洲寺庙的特色还是比比皆是。主庙前两侧，数头金色大象昂然伫立；入口广场上，一尊少年金身神像讲述着修行故事，神像旁的香案更让人感受到与[**华人**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/华人)寺庙文化的共鸣。

这座寺庙不仅是宗教殿堂，也是文化的交流中心，来自各地的游客络绎不绝，或驻足凝望，或低声祈祷。出来时，我们在烧香台旁驻足，尽管对印度教所知甚少，但仍旧点燃香火。随着那袅袅轻烟升腾，一种宁静悠远的氛围弥漫开来。这场旅程，从视觉的震撼，最终归于心灵的安宁。

## English Translation
# Golden Hair Dog

**Date:** 2025-12-23 01:00 ET  
**Author:** Peng Lihe  

Initially, when I heard people talk about Indian temples, images of majestic temples and golden deity statues would float into my mind, much like the Lingyin Temple in Hangzhou that I have visited many times—solemn, grand, and shrouded in swirling incense smoke, inspiring deep reverence. However, the BAPS Shri Swaminarayan Mandir in New Jersey is a completely different sight.

This marble structure is completely snow-white with fluid lines. It is both majestic and solemn, yet pure and ethereal, with an exterior that resembles a European cathedral, earning it the reputation of a "marble wonder."

One summer day, we drove here. As soon as we got out of the car, what caught our eyes were the pink walls and archway, followed by a gently rippling pool with several small fountains lightly splashing water. Surrounding the pool were green plants and red-brick paths, while melodious Vedic chanting echoed between heaven and earth, creating a tranquil atmosphere. In the welcome center, staff reminded visitors of the dress code, requiring sleeved tops and pants or skirts that cover the knees.

On both sides of the plaza leading to the main temple are pink corridors. The pillars are carved with heavenly musicians holding different instruments, each in a unique posture and with a vivid expression. The towering spires of the main temple pierce the clouds, shining white and dazzling in the sunlight, magnificent and imposing, like a palace of stone.

Entering the temple requires taking off one's shoes. I took off my sandals and stepped barefoot onto the marble floor; the cold sensation made me a bit uncomfortable at first. The administrator smiled and explained that walking barefoot allows one to better feel the holiness and energy of the temple. I tried taking a few steps, but ultimately went back to the car to put on socks, a round trip that took over half an hour.

Photography is prohibited inside the main temple, but this restriction actually brought a deeper sense of calm to my mind. The quiet atmosphere, soft light and shadows, and exquisite carvings blended perfectly to create an otherworldly experience. Besides the divine statues, the intricate relief carvings on the pillars and stone walls were also captivating.

These reliefs are exquisitely crafted, depicting complex scenes with natural and expressive figures, silently telling the stories of past sages. Overhead, the lotus-patterned carvings on the ceiling bloom layer by layer, while the central dome is the most spectacular, with its overlapping geometric structures looking like cosmic order frozen in mid-air.

This modern, grand building was assembled in the United States from thousands of marble blocks pre-carved in India. The statues in the main and side halls together construct the rich, sacred world of the Swaminarayan sect, with the supreme deity being Swaminarayan himself. This sage embarked on a seven-year pilgrimage and ascetic journey of over 12,000 kilometers during his youth, ultimately establishing this religious tradition in western India.

This history could not help but remind me of "Siddhartha" by Hermann Hesse and the story of Shakyamuni Buddha. Although Swaminarayan and Shakyamuni were separated by more than two thousand years—one establishing Buddhism, the other inheriting and reforming the Vaishnavism tradition of Hinduism—both resolutely abandoned secular life in their youth to embark on a path of spiritual practice, ultimately becoming founders of major religions. Both emphasized discipline and morality, and advocated for the equality of all living beings. Their life paths, transcending time and space, radiate a similar, pure brilliance.

Although this snow-white marble hall gives off the elegant feel of a European church, features of Asian temples are still found everywhere. In front of the main temple, several golden elephants stand proudly on both sides; in the entrance plaza, a golden statue of a youth narrates the story of spiritual practice, and the incense burner next to the statue makes one feel a resonance with Chinese temple culture.

This temple is not only a religious hall but also a center for cultural exchange, drawing a continuous stream of tourists from all over who stop to gaze or pray in low voices. As we left, we paused by the incense-burning station. Although we knew very little about Hinduism, we still lit some incense. As the wisps of light smoke rose, a quiet and far-reaching atmosphere spread. This journey, beginning with visual awe, ultimately returned to peace of mind.
