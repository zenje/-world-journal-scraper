# 我的婆母(上)

**Date:** 2026-01-10 01:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷

清晰地记得一九九二年[**春节**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/春节)临近，我从上海北站混在农民工的队伍里，挤上了去安徽滁县的一列火车，那正是农民工辛苦在外打工一年，回家乡和全家团聚过年的时刻，也正是中国[**春运**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/春运)高峰时期。

我手中拿了一个旅行袋，肩上斜跨着手提包，旅行袋有几件换洗的内衣之外，重要的是有八十六条相嵌著白色花朵的白色手帕，这是我要回到婆婆的老家，为婆婆奔丧准备的东西。我泪不泣声，赶紧把八十六条手帕卷成了八十六朵类似玫瑰花的模样，制成了一个特殊的花圈，敬献给我八十六岁离世的婆婆，止不住的泪水一滴滴点在玫瑰花的手帕上。

我一面做着玫瑰花，一边回忆着婆婆的一生。

我的婆婆是安徽省寿县城里孙姓财主家的独生女儿，自幼是父母的掌上明珠、心肝宝贝。婆婆刚学会走路不久，那时候封建传统仍在延续，她的母亲只能狠心咬牙帮女儿缠起了小脚，那缠脚布每隔几天要收紧一寸长，痛得婆婆大喊大叫。

婆婆从此没有胃口吃饭，也不肯玩，她的父亲对母亲说：「孩子痛苦成那样，我们就不要缠小脚了，将来如果嫁不出去，家里的财产可以供她过几辈子的。」由于父亲发话，婆婆的脚松绑了，虽有点变形，但比起缠过脚的要大得多，获得了一双半大不小的脚丫子。

婆婆小时候身边有两个贴身的丫头和一个奶妈，婆婆称奶妈为老干，老干照顾生活起居，两个丫头专门陪着玩耍，也要陪着读书。婆婆的父亲请来有学问、有修养的老师给她上课，也教一些有关女孩子应该具备的知识和礼节。

婆婆家中雇有两个长工，其中一个长工的儿子名叫多善，从小就聪明懂事、乖巧伶俐，知道自己应该站在什么位置上。婆婆的母亲很喜欢这孩子，自己家无男孩，就把多善过继为孙子，多善叫婆婆「姑姑」，这个比婆婆小三岁的多善还会哄着婆婆玩呢，嘴上不停地叫着：「姑姑、姑姑，我会抓蝴蝶，我会捉蜻蜓，抓到了都送给妳……。」

当时的农村是很闭塞的，一个村子里几十户人家，张三李四家家户户都很清楚，女孩子长大成人之后，就在本村选婚嫁娶。婆婆到了十六岁那年，她父母亲开始着急起来，为女儿找个门当户对的婚姻是件大事情，但本村的农户没有一个可以考虑的。

一次，婆婆家的帐房先生到田地的边界处，视察今年的庄稼收成，正巧遇到边界对面程姓的管家，两人无意间提到了各自东家的小姐和少爷，是他们两人促成了婆婆和公公的婚姻。

这名程家的少爷在家排名第九，九少爷就是他专用的名字。程氏家族有四十顷耕田，九少爷是老爷第五房太太所生，除了大哥、二哥比较会理财管家，另外六个兄长个个都是好吃懒做，吃喝嫖赌样样精通。

九少爷十七岁那年和十六岁的婆婆订婚，婆婆娘家立即动手置办嫁妆，什么床上用品，如绫罗绸缎面料的丝棉被、鸭绒被、绣花枕头，或四季服装、鞋袜帽子，还有夏天搧凉的檀香扇、鹅毛扇，冬季取暖的手烘笼、脚笼、放在被窝里的烫焐子……，穿的用的样样俱全；陪嫁的还有金银首饰、珠宝挂件、翡翠头筛、名贵字画、古代收藏，另外再加一大箱金叶子、金元宝……，真有点像[**京剧**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/京剧)「锁鏻囊」中富贵小姐出阁一样的排场。

（上）

## English Translation
My Mother-in-Law (Part 1)

Date: 2026-01-10 01:00 ET
Author: Peng Lihe

I clearly remember that as the Spring Festival of 1992 drew near, I blended into the crowd of migrant workers at the Shanghai North Railway Station and squeezed onto a train heading to Chuxian, Anhui. It was the exact moment when migrant workers, having worked hard away from home for a whole year, were returning to their hometowns to reunite with their families for the New Year, and it was also the peak of China's Spring Festival travel rush (Chunyun).

In my hand, I held a travel bag, and a handbag was slung across my shoulder. Aside from a few changes of undergarments, the travel bag contained something of great importance: eighty-six white handkerchiefs embroidered with white flowers. These were prepared for my return to my mother-in-law's hometown to attend her funeral. Choking back silent tears, I quickly rolled the eighty-six handkerchiefs into eighty-six rose-like shapes, creating a special wreath to offer to my mother-in-law, who had passed away at the age of eighty-six. Unstoppable tears dripped one by one onto the rose-shaped handkerchiefs.

As I fashioned the roses, I recalled my mother-in-law's life.

My mother-in-law was the only daughter of a wealthy landlord surnamed Sun in the county seat of Shou County, Anhui Province. From early childhood, she was the apple of her parents' eye, their precious darling. Shortly after she learned to walk, feudal traditions were still persisting, so her mother had no choice but to steel her heart and bind her daughter's feet. The foot-binding cloth had to be tightened by an inch every few days, making my mother-in-law scream in agony.

From then on, my mother-in-law lost her appetite and refused to play. Her father said to her mother: "Since the child is in such agony, let's not bind her feet. If she cannot marry in the future, the family fortune can sustain her for several lifetimes." Thanks to her father's intervention, her feet were unbound. Although they were slightly deformed, they were much larger than fully bound feet, leaving her with a pair of medium-sized feet.

When my mother-in-law was a child, she had two personal maidservants and a wet nurse by her side. She called the wet nurse "Lao Gan," who took care of her daily needs, while the two maidservants were dedicated to playing and studying with her. Her father hired a scholarly and cultured tutor to give her lessons, which also included the knowledge and etiquette that a young girl should possess.

Two long-term laborers were employed in her family's household. The son of one of them was named Duoshan. From an early age, he was intelligent, sensible, clever, and quick-witted, knowing exactly where he stood. My mother-in-law's mother liked the boy very much, and since their own family had no boys, they adopted Duoshan as a grandson. Duoshan called my mother-in-law "Auntie." Although he was three years younger than her, he knew how to coax her into playing, constantly calling out: "Auntie, Auntie, I can catch butterflies and dragonflies, and I'll give them all to you when I catch them..."

At that time, the countryside was very isolated. In a village of just a few dozen households, everyone knew their neighbors inside out. Once girls grew up, marriages were arranged within the village. When my mother-in-law turned sixteen, her parents began to worry. Finding a marriage of equal social status for their daughter was a major affair, yet not a single farming household in the village was worthy of consideration.

Once, the accountant of my mother-in-law's family went to the edge of the fields to inspect the year's harvest. He happened to run into the housekeeper of a family surnamed Cheng from across the border. The two casually mentioned their respective masters' daughter and son, and it was these two who brought about the marriage of my mother-in-law and father-in-law.

This young master of the Cheng family ranked ninth in the household, and "Ninth Young Master" was his exclusive moniker. The Cheng family owned forty qing (around 660 acres) of arable land, and the Ninth Young Master was born to the master's fifth wife. Except for the eldest and second brothers, who were relatively good at managing finances and running the household, the other six elder brothers were all lazy, gluttonous, and well-versed in drinking, gambling, and visiting prostitutes.

When the Ninth Young Master was seventeen, he became engaged to my sixteen-year-old mother-in-law. Her family immediately set to work preparing her dowry. There was bedding, such as silk-covered silk-cotton quilts, down duvets, and embroidered pillows; clothing, shoes, socks, and hats for all four seasons; and sandalwood and goose-feather fans for cooling off in the summer, hand-warmer cages, foot-warmer cages, and bed-warming vessels for the winter—every imaginable item for wear and use was provided. The dowry also included gold and silver jewelry, gem pendants, jade hair ornaments, valuable calligraphy and paintings, antique collections, and a large chest filled with gold leaves and gold ingots... Indeed, it was quite like the grand spectacle of a wealthy young lady's wedding in the Peking Opera "The Pocket of Riches and Honor" (Suolin Nang).

(Part 1)
