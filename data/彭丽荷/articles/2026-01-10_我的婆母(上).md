# 我的婆母(上)

**Date:** 2026-01-10 01:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷
**URL:** https://www.worldjournal.com/wj/story/121251/9244661?zh-cn

清晰地记得一九九二年[**春节**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/春节)临近，我从上海北站混在农民工的队伍里，挤上了去安徽滁县的一列火车，那正是农民工辛苦在外打工一年，回家乡和全家团聚过年的时刻，也正是中国[**春运**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/春运)高峰时期。

我手中拿了一个旅行袋，肩上斜跨着手提包，旅行袋有几件换洗的内衣之外，重要的是有八十六条相嵌著白色花朵的白色手帕，这是我要回到婆婆的老家，为婆婆奔丧准备的东西。我泪不泣声，赶紧把八十六条手帕卷成了八十六朵类似玫瑰花的模样，制成了一个特殊的花圈，敬献给我八十六岁离世的婆婆，止不住的泪水一滴滴点在玫瑰花的手帕上。

我一面做着玫瑰花，一边回忆着婆婆的一生。

我的婆婆是安徽省寿县城里孙姓财主家的独生女儿，自幼是父母的掌上明珠、心肝宝贝。婆婆刚学会走路不久，那时候封建传统仍在延续，她的母亲只能狠心咬牙帮女儿缠起了小脚，那缠脚布每隔几天要收紧一寸长，痛得婆婆大喊大叫。

婆婆从此没有胃口吃饭，也不肯玩，她的父亲对母亲说：「孩子痛苦成那样，我们就不要缠小脚了，将来如果嫁不出去，家里的财产可以供她过几辈子的。」由于父亲发话，婆婆的脚松绑了，虽有点变形，但比起缠过脚的要大得多，获得了一双半大不小的脚丫子。

婆婆小时候身边有两个贴身的丫头和一个奶妈，婆婆称奶妈为老干，老干照顾生活起居，两个丫头专门陪着玩耍，也要陪着读书。婆婆的父亲请来有学问、有修养的老师给她上课，也教一些有关女孩子应该具备的知识和礼节。

婆婆家中雇有两个长工，其中一个长工的儿子名叫多善，从小就聪明懂事、乖巧伶俐，知道自己应该站在什么位置上。婆婆的母亲很喜欢这孩子，自己家无男孩，就把多善过继为孙子，多善叫婆婆「姑姑」，这个比婆婆小三岁的多善还会哄着婆婆玩呢，嘴上不停地叫着：「姑姑、姑姑，我会抓蝴蝶，我会捉蜻蜓，抓到了都送给妳……。」

当时的农村是很闭塞的，一个村子里几十户人家，张三李四家家户户都很清楚，女孩子长大成人之后，就在本村选婚嫁娶。婆婆到了十六岁那年，她父母亲开始着急起来，为女儿找个门当户对的婚姻是件大事情，但本村的农户没有一个可以考虑的。

一次，婆婆家的帐房先生到田地的边界处，视察今年的庄稼收成，正巧遇到边界对面程姓的管家，两人无意间提到了各自东家的小姐和少爷，是他们两人促成了婆婆和公公的婚姻。

这名程家的少爷在家排名第九，九少爷就是他专用的名字。程氏家族有四十顷耕田，九少爷是老爷第五房太太所生，除了大哥、二哥比较会理财管家，另外六个兄长个个都是好吃懒做，吃喝嫖赌样样精通。

九少爷十七岁那年和十六岁的婆婆订婚，婆婆娘家立即动手置办嫁妆，什么床上用品，如绫罗绸缎面料的丝棉被、鸭绒被、绣花枕头，或四季服装、鞋袜帽子，还有夏天搧凉的檀香扇、鹅毛扇，冬季取暖的手烘笼、脚笼、放在被窝里的烫焐子……，穿的用的样样俱全；陪嫁的还有金银首饰、珠宝挂件、翡翠头筛、名贵字画、古代收藏，另外再加一大箱金叶子、金元宝……，真有点像[**京剧**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/京剧)「锁鏻囊」中富贵小姐出阁一样的排场。

## My Mother-in-Law (Part 1)
I clearly remember that as the Spring Festival of 1992 drew near, I mingled with the crowd of migrant workers at Shanghai North Railway Station and squeezed onto a train bound for Chuxian, Anhui. It was the exact time of year when migrant workers, after a year of hard work away from home, were returning to their hometowns to reunite with their families for the Chinese New Year—the peak of China's "Chunyun" (Spring Festival travel rush).

In my hand, I held a travel bag, and a handbag was slung across my shoulder. Inside the travel bag, besides a few changes of undergarments, the most important items were eighty-six white handkerchiefs embroidered with white flowers. These were what I had prepared for my mother-in-law's funeral as I headed back to her hometown. Weeping silently, I quickly rolled the eighty-six handkerchiefs into eighty-six rose-like shapes to make a special wreath to dedicate to my mother-in-law, who had passed away at the age of eighty-six. Unstoppable tears dripped one by one onto the rose-shaped handkerchiefs.

As I crafted the roses, I reminisced about my mother-in-law's life.

My mother-in-law was the only daughter of a wealthy landlord named Sun in the city of Shouxian, Anhui Province. From early childhood, she was the pearl of her parents' eyes, their absolute darling. Not long after she learned to walk, since feudal traditions were still persisting at the time, her mother had no choice but to steel herself, grit her teeth, and bind her daughter's feet. The foot-binding bandages had to be tightened by an inch every few days, making my mother-in-law cry out in pain.

From then on, my mother-in-law lost her appetite and refused to play. Her father said to her mother, "Since the child is in such agony, let's not bind her feet. If she cannot marry in the future, the family fortune is enough to support her for several lifetimes." Thanks to her father's intervention, my mother-in-law's feet were unbound. Although they were slightly deformed, they were much larger than fully bound feet, leaving her with a pair of medium-sized feet.

When she was a child, she had two personal maidservants and a wet nurse by her side. She called her wet nurse "Lao Gan," who took care of her daily routine, while the two maidservants were dedicated to playing with her and accompanying her in her studies. Her father hired a learned and cultivated tutor to teach her, instructing her in the knowledge and etiquette a young lady should possess.

The family employed two long-term laborers. The son of one of them was named Duoshan. He was smart, sensible, and clever from a young age, knowing his place well. My mother-in-law's mother was very fond of the boy, and since the family had no boys of their own, they adopted Duoshan as a grandson. Duoshan called my mother-in-law "Auntie." Even though he was three years younger than her, he knew how to coax her and play with her, constantly chirping, "Auntie, Auntie, I can catch butterflies and dragonflies, and I'll give them all to you when I catch them..."

Rural areas back then were very isolated. A village consisted of only a few dozen households, and everyone knew each other's business inside out. When girls grew up, they would marry within the village. When my mother-in-law turned sixteen, her parents began to worry. Finding a match of equal social and economic status was a major affair, and none of the farming households in their own village could even be considered.

Once, the accountant of my mother-in-law's family went to the edge of their fields to inspect the year's harvest. By chance, he met the housekeeper of the Cheng family from the other side of the border. The two casually brought up the young lady and young master of their respective employers, and it was the two of them who brought about the marriage between my mother-in-law and father-in-law.

This young master of the Cheng family ranked ninth in his family, and "Ninth Young Master" was his exclusive moniker. The Cheng family owned forty qing (approx. 660 acres) of arable land. The Ninth Young Master was born to the master's fifth wife. Apart from the eldest and second brothers, who were quite good at managing finances and the household, the other six older brothers were all lazy gluttons, adept at eating, drinking, whoring, and gambling.

When the Ninth Young Master was seventeen, he became engaged to my sixteen-year-old mother-in-law. Her family immediately began preparing her dowry: bedding such as silk and satin quilts, down duvets, and embroidered pillows; clothing, shoes, socks, and hats for all four seasons; sandalwood and goose feather fans to cool down in summer; hand warmers, foot warmers, and hot water bottles placed in the bedding to keep warm in winter... they had everything to wear and use. Her dowry also included gold and silver jewelry, gem pendants, jade hairnets, valuable calligraphy and paintings, antique collections, and a large chest filled with gold leaves and ingots... It really resembled the grand spectacle of a wealthy young lady getting married in the Peking Opera "The Unicorn Purse."
