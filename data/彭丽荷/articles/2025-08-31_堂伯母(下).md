# 堂伯母(下)

**Date:** 2025-08-31 02:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷

细伙有个远房亲戚，看到堂伯母家的遭遇很是可怜，出于同情之心，把细伙从湖北葛店带到上海淅江路一家瓷器店里当店员。起初细伙勤劳肯干，起早摸黑一心一意地为瓷器店干活，也深得老板的好评与信任。

日子一久，他结交了一个经常到店里批发瓷器的小贩，小贩时常带点好吃的东西送给他，细伙有时寄钱回去给媳妇，小贩也会主动掏些钱借给他，叫他多寄点回去。细伙很感激他，他俩相处、说话都很投机，便成了好友。

有一次闲聊中，小贩试探着对细伙说：「你家老板赚钱不少，付给你的月薪才一点点，太不合理了。你拿点货出来，我代你销售，这样你也可以多得到一点报酬。」细伙一听，很害怕地说：「不行，不行，我不能做那么缺德的事。」

一次小贩来批货，细伙在发货途中，小贩多拿一枝碗（十个碗摞在一起为「一筒」，十筒碗捆绑在一起称「一枝」），向细伙眨眨眼，示意多拿一枝碗不要讲出来，这时细伙也只能装傻。事后，小贩拿了一叠钞票悄悄地塞进了细伙的口袋，经不住钱的诱惑，细伙最终在糖衣炮弹前投降了。

尝到了甜头，胃口大了，胆子也壮了，小贩出的坏主意更多了，他们计划晚上等老板一家在三楼沉睡的时候，开始偷碗。起先是细伙两枝三枝地从店里传到店外，小贩在外面接应拿走去买，里应外合，几分钟就完成了。细伙觉得钱来得太容易了，愈干愈来劲，愈偷愈多。

深夜开门时，偶尔会发出点响声，小贩带来几坨生猪油，叫细伙把猪油放到门槽的凹膛中，起到润滑的作用，这样开门关门不会发出任何细微的声音。后来小贩索性踏了黄鱼车来偷货，他们干得愈来愈不像样了。

当然，老板时常也发现怎么店里的货有缺少现象，但也没有很重视，因为货源充足，生意不错，没有把它放在心上。一个深夜，老板肚子不适，起身想喝点热水，刚走到二楼，听到有细微的窸窸窣窣声音，停下脚步，仔细观察，是一楼发出来的，他轻手轻脚抓了个现。

两人吓得浑身直抖，小贩赶紧骑车窜逃，细伙则跪在地上讨饶。老板也气得够呛，上气不接下气地说：「你这个狗东西，竟敢做出这般伤天害理的事情，看我怎样收拾你。」老板报警，细伙被带走，在审问期间，细伙也说不清楚偷了多少次、拿了多少货、收了多少钱。老板一不做二不休，直接把细伙告到法庭。

堂伯母知道此事痛不欲生，无比绝望，求老板网开一面，饶过她的儿子，并且还把细伙寄给她儿媳妇的钱统统拿出来赔不是。老板不依不饶，要法庭重判，最后劳教三年。细伙快要释放的前几个月，染上了重病，就此一命呜呼。

堂伯母得到消息，五雷轰顶，一下子瘫坐在地上，又昏厥过去，不省人事。家中什么都没有了，只剩下可怜的三个寡妇，堂伯母说自己的命运比鲁迅笔下的祥林嫂还要苦几倍。

## English Translation
My Grand-Aunt (Part II)

Xi-huo had a distant relative who felt deeply sorry for his grand-aunt's tragic plight. Out of sympathy, this relative brought Xi-huo from Gedian, Hubei, to work as a shop assistant in a porcelain shop on Zhejiang Road in Shanghai. At first, Xi-huo was hardworking and willing to do any chore, working from dawn to dusk and devoting himself entirely to the porcelain shop, which won him high praise and trust from the owner.

As time went on, he befriended a peddler who frequently came to the shop to buy porcelain wholesale. The peddler would often bring him delicious treats. Sometimes, when Xi-huo sent money back to his wife, the peddler would offer to lend him cash so he could send more. Xi-huo was deeply grateful to him. The two got along well and enjoyed talking to each other, soon becoming close friends.

During a casual chat once, the peddler probed Xi-huo, saying, "Your boss makes a fortune, yet he pays you such a measly monthly wage. It's totally unfair. Why don't you slip out some merchandise? I can sell it for you, and that way you can get a little extra compensation." Upon hearing this, Xi-huo said fearfully, "No, no, I can't do something so wicked."

Once, when the peddler came to wholesale some goods, while Xi-huo was dispatching them, the peddler slipped an extra zhi of bowls (ten bowls stacked together make a 'tong,' and ten tong tied together are called a 'zhi'). The peddler winked at Xi-huo, signaling him not to say anything, and Xi-huo could only play dumb. Afterwards, the peddler quietly stuffed a stack of bills into Xi-huo's pocket. Unable to resist the temptation of money, Xi-huo finally surrendered to the sugar-coated bullets.

Having tasted the sweetness of easy money, his appetite grew, and his courage swelled. The peddler came up with even more wicked ideas. They planned to steal bowls at night while the owner's family was fast asleep on the third floor. At first, Xi-huo would pass two or three zhi of bowls from inside the shop to the outside, where the peddler would receive them and take them away to sell. Working in collusion from inside and out, they finished the job in just a few minutes. Xi-huo felt that money came too easily; the more he did it, the more enthusiastic he became, and the more he stole.

Since opening the door late at night occasionally made a bit of noise, the peddler brought a few lumps of raw lard and told Xi-huo to rub them inside the door tracks to lubricate them, ensuring that opening and closing the door wouldn't make the slightest sound. Later on, the peddler simply rode a three-wheeled cargo cart to steal the goods, and their operations became increasingly brazen and unseemly.

Of course, the boss occasionally noticed that goods went missing in the shop, but he didn't pay much attention because supply was plentiful and business was good, so he didn't take it to heart. One late night, the boss felt some stomach discomfort and got up to get some hot water. Just as he reached the second floor, he heard a faint rustling sound. He stopped in his tracks, listened carefully, and realized it was coming from the first floor. Tiptoeing down, he caught them red-handed.

The two of them trembled with fear. The peddler quickly fled on his cart, while Xi-huo fell to his knees, begging for mercy. The boss was absolutely furious and gasped for breath, shouting, "You wretched dog! How dare you do something so outrageous and wicked? Just wait and see how I deal with you!" The boss called the police, and Xi-huo was taken away. During the interrogation, Xi-huo couldn't even clearly state how many times he had stolen, how many goods he had taken, or how much money he had received. Deciding to go all the way, the boss took Xi-huo straight to court.

Upon hearing this news, Xi-huo's mother was utterly devastated and filled with despair. She begged the boss to show mercy and spare her son, even taking out all the money Xi-huo had sent back to his wife to offer as compensation and an apology. However, the boss remained unforgiving and demanded a harsh sentence from the court; in the end, Xi-huo was sentenced to three years of re-education through labor. Just a few months before his scheduled release, Xi-huo contracted a serious illness and passed away.

Hearing the news of his death was like a bolt from the blue to his mother. She collapsed onto the ground, fainted, and became unconscious. With nothing left in the house, only three pitiful widows remained. She lamented that her destiny was several times more bitter than that of Xianglin's Wife, the tragic character penned by Lu Xun.
