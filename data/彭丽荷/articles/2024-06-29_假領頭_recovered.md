# 假領頭

**Date:** 2024-06-29 02:00 ET
**Author:** 彭麗荷
**URL:** https://www.worldjournal.com/wj/story/121251/8047690

  

外孙女囡囡问我：「阿婆，您有白衬衫吗？」我拿出一件是绸缎的，一件是的确凉（涤纶）的，两件白衬衫，让她随意挑选。她选中了的确凉的那件，对我说：「阿婆，我要把这件衬衫剪去两只袖子，再把衬衫的下半身也剪掉，只剩半截带领头的衣服。」

我非常诧异地问她：「这么好的衣服，为什么要剪成那个样子？」她回答说：「我们班级要举行一个演唱会，表演的服装有统一要求。我们的节目，有六个同学上台表演，每个同学都要穿上红色圆领衫、蓝裤子，配上白色假领头。这三种颜色搭配在一起，鲜艳亮丽、正气凛然，代表美国星条旗上的红、白、蓝三种颜色。」

听完囡囡振振有词的说法，一方面觉得有道理，但另外又不忍心把我喜欢的衬衫剪坏。我说：「这件衬衫，还很新呢！怎么可以剪掉它，只剩个领头，这我不愿意。」一件半新的衬衫要随意把它剪掉，多可惜呀！我打心里不舍得，没有答应囡囡的要求，但是我又怕她不高兴，所以时时观察她的动向。

两天以后，我看到囡囡的写字台上放了一个洁白、全新的半截带领头的「残缺衣服」。原来囡囡花了自己的零用钱，去商店买回一件全新的白衬衫，然后自己动手剪成了一个假领头（见图，作者提供）。囡囡穿戴好以后，在镜子面前反复摆拢著，这样看看、那样瞧瞧，感到很得意洋洋，满脸笑容。她不但没有生我的气，还高兴地问我：「好看吗？阿婆。」我感到不可思议，心想这里的孩子没吃过苦，大手大脚地乱搞一通，只是上台表演，亮相几分钟的时间，就糟遢了一件完好的新衬衫。

看到了囡囡这番操作，我还真的有点动情。记得上世纪六、七○年代，那时的衣服都得凭布票，每人每年只有一丈多布票，买了上身的衣服，就没有下身的裤子，床单、枕套、毛巾之类的，能将就的，统统克服一下吧！

不知道是哪位上海人开了脑洞，创造发明了假领头。故名思义，这假领头只需要少量的布即可做成，它不用袖子，也没有整件衣服，只需要一个领子，可以节省很多布料。领子下面大约有一、两寸的衣襟，左右两边的衣襟上，缝两条绳子，放在双肩上，调整好尺度，把绳子在腋下打个结，再把假领头固定好位子，扭好领头上的两颗扣子，整理一下，在领子的外面套上圆领的汗衫或者棉毛衫，挺刮的领子翻在外面。对著镜子，乍一看，还挺精神、气派的呢！不知情的人误以为这个穿假领头的人，真有派头，很有风度；而穿戴假领头的人们沾沾自喜，也觉得自己很不错、很神气。

那时，我们没有洗衣机，大小衣服都得人工用手来搓洗，假领头体积小，洗起来很方便，同时可以节省用水。肥皂也是凭票供应的，这样肥皂也很耐用，总之什么东西都要考虑节约。假领头是受到人们欢迎和青睐的装饰衣。

那时，我们正处在三年自然灾害时期，缺衣少食，有个假领子套在脖子上就非常满足了，它不但节省了布料，还帮助大家装饰了漂亮的门面，长了臭面子。备有三、四个假领子的，不管谁，都会感到无限幸福和快乐，因为两三天就可以更换一次不同色彩的「衬衣」。领头不断地更换，让自己和别人都有一种新鲜感，心情特别美好！走起路来挺胸昂首，神气活现，那种臭美的感觉至今还留在我的记忆中。

一下子，假领头火了一座城，马路上的男女老少头颈下面都穿戴著「擦刮辣新」（上海话，表示又新又挺的意思）的假领头。上海南京路上就连最高档的「开开」衬衫店，橱窗里也摆满了各种面料、不同颜色、大小规格的假领头。接著，每个人都配戴假领头的风潮，席卷了全国。

一九七二年二月，美国总统尼克森访华。我记得在尼克森总统将要来中国的前一个月，上海的环卫工人把马路打理得干干净净，商店的橱柜、窗子擦得可以照亮人，不留一丝痕迹、一粒灰尘。然而，橱窗里的假领头全部暂时「撤退」，一个也见不到了，它们集体「下岗」，都到仓库里去「休息」。全新、完整的、端庄耀眼的真实衬衫闪亮登场，正式「上岗」。

八、九○年代中国改革开放，经济逐渐发展、提升，市场上的物质丰富了，老百姓的生活得到了改善，废除了买东西的各种票证，衣服随身买，食品随意挑。假领头烟消云散，再也不见了！

## The Fake Collar
My granddaughter Nannan asked me, "Grandma, do you have a white shirt?" I pulled out two white shirts—one made of silk and the other of Dacron (polyester)—and let her choose. She picked the Dacron one and said, "Grandma, I want to cut off the sleeves of this shirt, and then cut off the bottom half too, so only the top half with the collar is left."

Highly surprised, I asked her, "Why would you cut up such a nice shirt like that?" She replied, "Our class is putting on a concert, and there's a uniform dress code for the performance. In our act, six students will perform on stage, and each of us needs to wear a red crewneck t-shirt, blue pants, and a white fake collar. These three colors matched together are bright, beautiful, and inspiring, representing the red, white, and blue of the American flag."

Hearing Nannan's plausible argument, I felt she had a point on one hand, but on the other hand, I couldn't bear to see my favorite shirt ruined. I said, "This shirt is still quite new! How can you just cut it up until only the collar is left? I won't allow it." What a waste to casually cut up a half-new shirt! Deep down, I couldn't bring myself to do it, so I refused her request. However, worrying she might be upset, I kept a close eye on her.

Two days later, I saw a pristine, brand-new, half-cut "damaged garment" with a collar sitting on Nannan's desk. It turned out she had used her own pocket money to buy a brand-new white shirt from the store, and then personally cut it into a fake collar. Once she put it on, she kept adjusting it in front of the mirror, looking at it this way and that, looking extremely proud of herself with a wide grin. Instead of being mad at me, she cheerfully asked, "Does it look good, Grandma?" I found it mind-boggling. I thought to myself that kids here have never known hardship; they spend money extravagantly and act recklessly. Just for a few minutes of stage time, she had ruined a perfectly good new shirt.

Seeing Nannan's actions truly stirred my emotions. I remember back in the 1960s and 70s, clothing could only be bought with fabric coupons. Each person was allotted just over one zhang (about 3.3 meters) of fabric coupons per year. If you bought fabric for a top, you wouldn't have enough for pants. Things like bedsheets, pillowcases, and towels had to be made do with—we just had to overcome the difficulties and get by!

I don’t know which Shanghainese person had the stroke of genius to invent the fake collar. True to its name, this fake collar required only a tiny amount of fabric to make. It had no sleeves and no body—just the collar. This saved a huge amount of material. Extending about one or two inches below the collar were front flaps, with two strings sewn onto the left and right flaps. You would drape them over your shoulders, adjust the fit, tie the strings under your armpits to secure the fake collar in place, button up the two buttons on the collar, adjust it, and then put a crewneck undershirt or thermal top over it, leaving the crisp collar folded over the outside. Looking in the mirror, at first glance, it looked quite smart and elegant! People who didn’t know any better would assume the person wearing the fake collar had real style and poise, while those wearing them felt smug and thought they looked quite dashing and grand.

Back then, we didn't have washing machines, so all clothes, large and small, had to be scrubbed by hand. Because the fake collar was so small, it was very convenient to wash and saved water. Soap was also rationed with coupons, so this made the soap last longer too. In short, everything had to be done with conservation in mind. The fake collar was a decorative garment welcomed and favored by everyone.

At that time, we were in the midst of the Three Years of Natural Disasters period, short on food and clothing. Just having a fake collar around our necks brought immense satisfaction. It not only saved fabric but also helped people keep up appearances and vanity. Anyone who owned three or four fake collars felt boundlessly happy and fortunate, because they could change into a different colored "shirt" every two or three days. Constantly changing collars gave oneself and others a sense of freshness, making one's mood exceptionally wonderful! Walking with chest out and head held high, looking full of vigor—that feeling of vanity still lingers in my memory today.

All of a sudden, the fake collar took the city by storm. On the streets, young and old, men and women, all wore "ca-gua-la-xin" (a Shanghainese slang meaning brand new and crisp) fake collars under their necks. Even the display window of the most high-end shirt shop on Nanjing Road in Shanghai, "Kai Kai," was filled with fake collars of various fabrics, colors, and sizes. Soon after, the trend of everyone wearing fake collars swept across the nation.

In February 1972, US President Richard Nixon visited China. I remember that a month before President Nixon was to arrive in China, Shanghai's sanitation workers cleaned the streets spotlessly, and the display cabinets and windows of shops were wiped clean enough to reflect people like mirrors, without leaving a trace of smudge or a speck of dust. However, the fake collars in the windows all made a temporary "retreat" and were nowhere to be seen. They collectively "went off-duty" to "rest" in the warehouses. Brand new, complete, dignified, and dazzling real shirts made a grand entrance, officially "taking over the shift."

In the 1980s and 90s, with China's reform and opening up, the economy gradually grew and upgraded. Goods on the market became abundant, and ordinary people's lives improved. The various coupons for buying goods were abolished; clothes could be bought at will, and food could be chosen freely. The fake collar vanished into thin air, never to be seen again!
