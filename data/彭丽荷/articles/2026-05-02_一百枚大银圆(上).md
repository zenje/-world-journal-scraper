# 一百枚大银圆(上)

**Date:** 2026-05-02 02:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷

那年我六岁，身穿白底带有各种颜色花朵图案的洋布连衣裙，后脑勺左右扎上一对羊角辫，辫子上用大红的绸丝带系了两个蝴蝶结，肩膀上斜挎一个红色、小巧玲珑的皮包包。在镜子前照了照，觉得自己好美呀，得意得不行。

祖母拽着我的小手，生怕我走丢了，祖孙俩快步地朝十六浦码头赶去，说是去乘坐长江轮船，到湖北葛店老家走走，看望祖母生活过几十年的故土和老屋。赶路过程中，我连走带跑地跟着祖母前行，两根小辫子伴随着蝴蝶结在空中起舞。我既高兴又激动，有点紧张，也有点懵懂。

走上了「江汉」大轮船，来到了四等舱的船位上，我们对号入床后把行李塞到床铺下面，我和祖母合睡一张床。我身高没有超过一米，所以只需交十分之一的票价。

大轮船行驶在长江上，上海到[**武汉**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/武汉)是逆流，因此要在船上住三天三夜。每日三餐，有服务员推着车子，里面有盒饭和荤素菜类、面条[**饺子**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/饺子)及各类烧饼，只需付钱就可以领到想要的食物，也可以到船头的餐厅去用一些高档的膳食。

上船后的第一天，我觉得什么都那么新鲜、有趣，哪儿都想去见识见识。我小心翼翼走出船舱外，东瞧瞧、西看看，抓紧船上的栏杆，身子略微倾斜，俯瞰了好久好久。

带有浅浅土黄色的长江之水滚滚向东流，眺望远处，一望无边无际的天和地连接成一条无限长度的线条，场景非常壮观。轮船在水面上前行，整个水面又像铺天盖地的一张庞大豆腐皮，来往的船只行走在豆腐皮上，马上开出了一道宽畅的信道，让船只稳步前进。

第二天的清晨，祖母牵着我走到船头的甲板上，看到一轮闪烁红色的光亮在天和水接连处缓缓地升起，世界呈现出灿烂媚美的光彩，美丽过人，整个天空光辉夺目。

大轮船停靠在武汉码头的港湾水域里，祖母一眼就看出站在码头上来迎接我们的小姑子（我的姑婆），她正挥着高高举起的手向我们示意。旁边还有一个看上去老实憨厚的中年男人，手扶着一辆独轮车，满脸笑容地看着我们。

祖母、姑婆、我三人和行李都坐上了独轮车，车子中央只有一个很大的轮盘，两旁有木板架在车子上，可以坐人。然后用粗长的稻草绳把我们和行李分别绑牢在独轮车的两边，又见那中年人用独轮车扶手架上系着的绳索斜跨在自己肩上，将独轮车用劲一擡、起步，独轮车轴心开始转动，车子向前缓步行驶，发出了吱吱嘎嘎的响声。祖母紧紧地搂住我，叫我别害怕，一会儿就到家了。我从来未见到这般车子，只觉得有点滑稽也有些害怕。

大约走了两、三个时辰，我们进入了村庄，祖母对小姑子说：「我和荷荷应该去彭家墩住，那里的嫂子、弟妹都等着我们呢。」姑婆听了便大声嚷嚷：「那不行，嫂子，您是我最亲的人，我打小就是您和二哥陪伴我长大成人的，这次您回来一定要住我这里，我还有重要的事情告诉您。」

姑婆是个没有出嫁的老姑娘，父母走后，由二哥二嫂（我祖父、祖母）照看和抚养。她的性情略微古怪，虽然家中有很多来提亲的，都被她一一拒绝。

一天，她对二哥说，不想一直住在彭家墩，想自己单住，我祖父就在邻近的村子「小豇豆」置了房子，也分些田地在她名下，并安排了两名长工帮助她管理田地的农活。姑婆就独自一人在「小豇豆」生活着，她勤俭持家，省吃俭用，很会攒钱，平日里只吃点咸菜酸瓜，过得很清贫，但对别人倒不是太抠门。

祖母拗不过，而姑婆又是那么真诚和热情地坚持着。我和祖母走进了姑婆的家，天色已是暗淡，我们早早上床息休，由于一天的劳累，我头碰到了枕头就酣酣大睡起来。（上）

## English Translation
One Hundred Large Silver Dollars (Part 1)

Date: 2026-05-02 02:00 ET
Author: Peng Lihe

That year, I was six years old. I wore a machine-woven cotton dress with a white background and colorful floral patterns. On the left and right sides of the back of my head, my hair was tied into twin pigtails, each adorned with a bow made of bright red silk ribbon. Slung across my shoulder was a small, delicate red leather purse. Looking in the mirror, I thought I looked so beautiful and was incredibly pleased with myself.

My grandmother held my little hand tightly, terrified that I might get lost. The two of us walked briskly toward Shiliupu Wharf, heading to catch a Yangtze River steamer to visit our ancestral home in Gedian, Hubei, to see the land and old house where my grandmother had lived for decades. Along the way, I jogged to keep up with her, my two pigtails and their bows dancing in the air. I was happy, excited, a little nervous, and somewhat bewildered.

We boarded the grand steamer "Jianghan" and made our way to our fourth-class cabin berths. After finding our assigned beds, we stuffed our luggage underneath. My grandmother and I shared one bed. Since I was under one meter tall, I only had to pay one-tenth of the ticket price.

The grand steamer traveled along the Yangtze River. Going from Shanghai to Wuhan meant traveling upstream, so we had to spend three days and three nights on board. For our three daily meals, servers would push carts filled with boxed lunches, meat and vegetable dishes, noodles, dumplings, and various baked cakes. We could get whatever food we wanted simply by paying, or we could go to the restaurant at the bow of the ship to enjoy higher-end meals.

On the first day aboard, I found everything so fresh and interesting; I wanted to explore everywhere. Cautiously, I stepped out of the cabin, peeking here and looking there. Grasping the ship’s railing, I leaned my body slightly forward and peered down for a very long time.

The pale, clay-yellow waters of the Yangtze rolled eastward. Looking out into the distance, the boundless sky and water merged into an infinitely long line—it was a truly magnificent sight. As the steamer surged forward, the entire surface of the water looked like an immense sheet of tofu skin blanketed across the earth. The ships passing to and fro moved upon this "tofu skin," instantly carving out wide, smooth pathways that allowed them to progress steadily.

On the morning of the second day, grandmother led me to the deck at the bow. We saw a shimmering red glow slowly rising at the junction of the sky and water. The world took on a brilliant, enchanting luster; it was extraordinarily beautiful, and the entire sky was dazzling.

The grand steamer docked in the harbor of the Wuhan wharf. With a single glance, my grandmother spotted her sister-in-law (my great-aunt) waiting on the dock to welcome us. She was waving her hand high in the air to catch our attention. Beside her stood an honest-looking, middle-aged man, holding a wheelbarrow and smiling warmly at us.

Grandmother, my great-aunt, and I, along with our luggage, all piled onto the wheelbarrow. The cart had only one large wheel in the center, with wooden frames on both sides for passengers to sit on. We and our luggage were secured tightly to either side of the wheelbarrow with thick straw ropes. Then, the middle-aged man slung the strap attached to the wheelbarrow handles diagonally across his shoulder. With a forceful lift, he set off. The axle of the wheelbarrow began to turn, and the cart moved slowly forward, making a creaking and squeaking sound. Grandmother held me tightly and told me not to be afraid, saying we would be home in no time. I had never seen such a vehicle before; I found it both amusing and a little scary.

After traveling for about two or three hours, we entered the village. Grandmother said to her sister-in-law, "Hehe and I should go stay at Pengjiadun. My sisters-in-law there are all waiting for us." Hearing this, Great-Aunt shouted loudly, "No way, sister-in-law! You are my dearest relative. I was raised by you and my second brother since I was a child. Since you're back this time, you must stay at my place. I also have something very important to tell you."

Great-Aunt was an unmarried spinster. After her parents passed away, she was cared for and raised by her second brother and sister-in-law (my grandparents). She had a slightly eccentric temperament; although many matchmakers had come to propose marriage, she had rejected them all one by one.

One day, she told her second brother that she didn't want to live in Pengjiadun forever and wanted to live on her own. My grandfather then purchased a house for her in the neighboring village of "Xiaojiangdou," registered some land in her name, and arranged for two hired laborers to help her manage the farmwork. Great-Aunt lived alone in "Xiaojiangdou." She managed her household with diligence and thrift, lived very frugally, and was excellent at saving money. On ordinary days, she only ate pickles and sour melons, living a very simple and impoverished life, but she was not too stingy when it came to others.

Grandmother couldn't win the argument, and Great-Aunt insisted with such sincerity and warmth. My grandmother and I walked into Great-Aunt's house. By then, the sky had already darkened. We went to bed early to rest. Exhausted from the day's journey, my head hit the pillow and I fell into a deep, sound sleep. (Part 1)
