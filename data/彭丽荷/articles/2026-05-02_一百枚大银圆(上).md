# 一百枚大银圆(上)

**Date:** 2026-05-02 02:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷
**URL:** https://www.worldjournal.com/wj/story/121251/9463718?zh-cn

那年我六岁，身穿白底带有各种颜色花朵图案的洋布连衣裙，后脑勺左右扎上一对羊角辫，辫子上用大红的绸丝带系了两个蝴蝶结，肩膀上斜挎一个红色、小巧玲珑的皮包包。在镜子前照了照，觉得自己好美呀，得意得不行。

祖母拽着我的小手，生怕我走丢了，祖孙俩快步地朝十六浦码头赶去，说是去乘坐长江轮船，到湖北葛店老家走走，看望祖母生活过几十年的故土和老屋。赶路过程中，我连走带跑地跟着祖母前行，两根小辫子伴随着蝴蝶结在空中起舞。我既高兴又激动，有点紧张，也有点懵懂。

走上了「江汉」大轮船，来到了四等舱的船位上，我们对号入床后把行李塞到床铺下面，我和祖母合睡一张床。我身高没有超过一米，所以只需交十分之一的票价。

大轮船行驶在长江上，上海到[**武汉**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/武汉)是逆流，因此要在船上住三天三夜。每日三餐，有服务员推着车子，里面有盒饭和荤素菜类、面条[**饺子**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/饺子)及各类烧饼，只需付钱就可以领到想要的食物，也可以到船头的餐厅去用一些高档的膳食。

上船后的第一天，我觉得什么都那么新鲜、有趣，哪儿都想去见识见识。我小心翼翼走出船舱外，东瞧瞧、西看看，抓紧船上的栏杆，身子略微倾斜，俯瞰了好久好久。

带有浅浅土黄色的长江之水滚滚向东流，眺望远处，一望无边无际的天和地连接成一条无限长度的线条，场景非常壮观。轮船在水面上前行，整个水面又像铺天盖地的一张庞大豆腐皮，来往的船只行走在豆腐皮上，马上开出了一道宽畅的信道，让船只稳步前进。

第二天的清晨，祖母牵着我走到船头的甲板上，看到一轮闪烁红色的光亮在天和水接连处缓缓地升起，世界呈现出灿烂媚美的光彩，美丽过人，整个天空光辉夺目。

大轮船停靠在武汉码头的港湾水域里，祖母一眼就看出站在码头上来迎接我们的小姑子（我的姑婆），她正挥着高高举起的手向我们示意。旁边还有一个看上去老实憨厚的中年男人，手扶着一辆独轮车，满脸笑容地看着我们。

祖母、姑婆、我三人和行李都坐上了独轮车，车子中央只有一个很大的轮盘，两旁有木板架在车子上，可以坐人。然后用粗长的稻草绳把我们和行李分别绑牢在独轮车的两边，又见那中年人用独轮车扶手架上系着的绳索斜跨在自己肩上，将独轮车用劲一擡、起步，独轮车轴心开始转动，车子向前缓步行驶，发出了吱吱嘎嘎的响声。祖母紧紧地搂住我，叫我别害怕，一会儿就到家了。我从来未见到这般车子，只觉得有点滑稽也有些害怕。

大约走了两、三个时辰，我们进入了村庄，祖母对小姑子说：「我和荷荷应该去彭家墩住，那里的嫂子、弟妹都等着我们呢。」姑婆听了便大声嚷嚷：「那不行，嫂子，您是我最亲的人，我打小就是您和二哥陪伴我长大成人的，这次您回来一定要住我这里，我还有重要的事情告诉您。」

姑婆是个没有出嫁的老姑娘，父母走后，由二哥二嫂（我祖父、祖母）照看和抚养。她的性情略微古怪，虽然家中有很多来提亲的，都被她一一拒绝。

一天，她对二哥说，不想一直住在彭家墩，想自己单住，我祖父就在邻近的村子「小豇豆」置了房子，也分些田地在她名下，并安排了两名长工帮助她管理田地的农活。姑婆就独自一人在「小豇豆」生活着，她勤俭持家，省吃俭用，很会攒钱，平日里只吃点咸菜酸瓜，过得很清贫，但对别人倒不是太抠门。

祖母拗不过，而姑婆又是那么真诚和热情地坚持着。我和祖母走进了姑婆的家，天色已是暗淡，我们早早上床息休，由于一天的劳累，我头碰到了枕头就酣酣大睡起来。（上）

## One Hundred Large Silver Dollars (Part 1)
That year I was six years old, wearing a calico dress with a white background and colorful floral patterns. I had a pair of pigtails tied on both sides of the back of my head, with two bowknots tied on them using bright red silk ribbons, and a small, delicate red leather bag slung across my shoulder. I looked at myself in the mirror and thought I was so beautiful, feeling incredibly pleased with myself.

My grandmother held tightly to my small hand, terrified that I would get lost, as the grandmother and grandchild hurried toward Shiliupu Pier. She said we were going to take a Yangtze River steamship to visit our ancestral home in Gedian, Hubei, to see the native land and old house where she had lived for decades. Along the way, I followed her by half-walking and half-running, my two little pigtails dancing in the air along with the bows. I was both happy and excited, a little nervous, and somewhat bewildered.

We boarded the grand steamship "Jianghan" and made our way to our berths in the fourth-class cabin. After finding our assigned beds, we tucked our luggage underneath. My grandmother and I shared a single bed. Since I was under one meter tall, my ticket only cost one-tenth of the regular fare.

The steamship sailed up the Yangtze River. Traveling from Shanghai to Wuhan meant going upstream, so we had to stay on board for three days and three nights. For our three daily meals, attendants pushed carts filled with boxed meals, meat and vegetable dishes, noodles, dumplings, and various baked clay-oven rolls. We could get any food we wanted just by paying, or we could head to the restaurant at the bow of the ship to enjoy some upscale dining.

On the first day aboard, everything felt so fresh and interesting; I wanted to explore and see it all. I stepped out of the cabin with great caution, looking around this way and that. Gripping the ship's railing tightly, I leaned my body slightly outward and looked down at the water for a long, long time.

The pale, yellowish waters of the Yangtze River rolled eastward. Gazing into the distance, the boundless sky and water merged into a line of infinite length, a truly magnificent sight. As the steamship pressed forward, the entire surface of the water looked like an enormous sheet of tofu skin blanketed across the world. The passing boats traveled over this tofu skin, instantly slicing open a wide, smooth channel for ships to advance steadily.

On the morning of the second day, grandmother led me to the deck at the bow. There, we watched a shimmering red glow slowly rise from where the sky met the water. The world was bathed in a brilliant and enchanting light, exquisitely beautiful, and the entire sky was dazzlingly radiant.

When the steamship docked in the harbor of the Wuhan pier, my grandmother immediately spotted her sister-in-law (my grand-aunt) standing there to welcome us. She was waving her high-raised hand to get our attention. Next to her was an honest and simple-looking middle-aged man holding a wheelbarrow, watching us with a face full of smiles.

My grandmother, my grand-aunt, myself, and our luggage all got onto the wheelbarrow. It had only one large wheel in the middle, with wooden boards mounted on both sides for seating. We and our luggage were then securely bound to either side of the wheelbarrow with thick, long straw ropes. The middle-aged man slung the harness rope tied to the wheelbarrow handles across his shoulder, lifted the wheelbarrow with a mighty heave, and set off. The wheelbarrow's axle began to turn as the vehicle slowly moved forward, emitting creaks and groans. Grandmother held me tight and told me not to be afraid, saying we would be home in a moment. I had never seen such a contraption before; I found it both amusingly silly and a little scary.

After traveling for about four to six hours, we entered the village. Grandmother said to her sister-in-law, "Hehe and I should stay at Pengjiadun; the sisters-in-law and younger siblings there are all waiting for us." Upon hearing this, my grand-aunt clamored loudly, "That won't do, Sister-in-law! You are my dearest relative. I was raised by you and Second Brother since I was a child. Now that you are back, you must stay at my place. I have something very important to tell you."

My grand-aunt was an unmarried spinster. After her parents passed away, she was cared for and raised by her second brother and his wife (my grandfather and grandmother). Her temperament was somewhat eccentric; though many matchmakers came to propose marriage, she turned them all down one by one.

One day, she told her second brother that she did not want to live in Pengjiadun forever and wished to live on her own. My grandfather then acquired a house for her in the neighboring village of "Xiaojiangdou," registered some farmland under her name, and arranged for two long-term hired laborers to help her manage the farm work. Thus, my grand-aunt lived all by herself in "Xiaojiangdou." She managed the household with industry and thrift, lived frugally, and was very good at saving money. On ordinary days, she ate only pickles and sour melons, living a very simple and impoverished life, though she was not too stingy toward others.

Grandmother could not change her mind, and my grand-aunt was so sincere and warm in her persistence. So, grandmother and I walked into my grand-aunt's home. By then, the sky had already darkened, and we went to bed early to rest. Exhausted from the day's journey, my head had barely hit the pillow before I fell into a deep, sound sleep. (Part 1)
