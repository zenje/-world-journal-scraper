# 「燈」又亮起

**Date:** 2024-09-28 02:00 ET
**Author:** 彭麗荷
**URL:** https://www.worldjournal.com/wj/story/121251/8241914

  

二十多年前，来到女儿家依亲。把箱子打开，从上海带来的土特产分发给女儿、女婿还有亲家，一阵热闹过后，我激动了好多天。在以后的一个月，女儿带我游览了[**旧金山**](http://web.archive.org/web/20241011084335/https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/旧金山)、[**洛杉矶**](http://web.archive.org/web/20241011084335/https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/洛杉矶)等各大城市。之后，女儿对我说，他们工作都很忙，不能每天陪我逛街、聊天的，家附近有个大公园，叫我每天早晨可以去散散步，找点朋友讲讲话、聊聊天。

我从一个熟悉、热闹的上海城市，来到了美国西部圣地牙哥城市生活，在这辽阔地大、人口稀少的土地上，周围的环境、小区、街道、商店和上海大不相同。虽然环境优美，小区绿化的像公园一样，可是，街道上看不到行人，街道两旁没有一家商店，买东西必须开车到市中心去买。一下子我还真有点儿适应不了，心想只能慢慢地磨合，逐渐习惯起来。

一天，我去公园，公园很大，可是没能见到几个人，只有两个白皮肤、蓝眼睛的洋人牵著小狗在溜达，我的英语水平又不够和他们交流。因此散步之后，一个人呆呆地坐在长椅上，东张西望无目的探索著什么。

忽然，面前迎来一对约莫五十来岁的夫妇，「Are you Chinese？（你是中国人吗？）」女士轻声地问道，她在跟我打招呼。我脸上带满了笑容答：「Yes.」，她听了我的回答，马上就转用不标准的北京方言跟我说：「太好了，我们是从台湾来的，我的先生姓汤。」我立即向他们称呼了一声：「汤先生、汤太太，今天遇到了中国朋友，我太高兴了！」

当他们知道我在为寂寞而发愁，他们想出了一个好办法，就是汤太太每天给我送来一份前一天的「[**世界日报**](http://web.archive.org/web/20241011084335/https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/世界日报)」，她并不太好意思地说：「报纸我们先阅读，第二天送给您看，虽然是隔一天的新闻，您也可以了解到很多世界上发生的大事，美国的国情和各种文艺方面的消息，这样能帮您解解闷。」

这下把我高兴坏了，有了报纸陪伴，我的精神一下子抖擞起来，精神上有了粮食，生活中增添了乐趣，心中亮起了一盏「灯」。

某天，我阅读了「家园」版的各类文章，旁边有一则温馨的的提告：「只要您拿起笔，您就是作家。」我看了后，似乎感受到阵阵鼓励，心想，自己是否也想当一回作家呢？于是，我给自己打了打气、壮了壮胆，思索了一番，提起笔写了第一篇小作文，以「灯」作为题目。

那是在二○○○年，当时还没有用电脑，没有网路，也没有电子邮件，全是手工操作。为了让字体清楚显示，我用打印机将字逐个打印出来，然后反复地阅读查看，每个字、每条句子就连标点符号都审查了好几遍，自己认为像一篇作文，有点意思，就大胆地装入信封，投寄到世界日报家园栏目中去。

我每天都在指望汤太太送来的隔天报纸，是否能在家园栏目中看到「灯」的作文出现？我不好意思告诉汤太太有关投稿的事情，如果作文没有被录用，我会很尴尬的。

我左等右盼地等待著。一天，汤太太对我说：「汤先生的工作有所变动，他要调动到洛杉矶总部的公司去了，我也要随著汤先生搬家了。」

汤太太和我告别时，我俩都依依不舍，我感谢她的关怀。但心中更是无奈，我不但见不到每天为我送报的好朋友，更是和世界日报失去了联系，「灯」这篇作文是否得到家园栏目认可、有否录用？一切都不得而知。当时思想上产生了一些情绪，我心中的「灯」是继续让它照亮我的世界？还是就此熄灭呢？

过了一段时间，见到我的亲家，告诉她上述的情况，她是个热心爽快的人，她说有个朋友家中订阅世界日报，每天都会收到报纸，她委托朋友帮助在家园栏目寻找「灯」一文。这位朋友翻查了一个多月前的报纸，好不容易找到了它，赶快把报纸递给我亲家，亲家又急忙从旧金山寄给我，费了不少功夫和周折。

经过多位的帮助，我终于看到了「灯」一文，在二○○○年九月三十日出现在家园版（见图），要知道我当时是多么兴奋！差一点就要手舞足蹈了。世界日报不但给我带来了快乐，而且还为我提供讲故事的平台。

我和「灯」一文有著深厚的情感，之后，我试著又陆续地写了一些「花」、「寿」、「亦师亦友」等作文，都得到了报社的肯定和录用。但是，我都无法知道是什么时候发表的，它被报纸印刷之后，长成什么样子，一概不知，直到收到稿酬后，才知道这篇被录用了，那篇还没有。

春去秋来，一晃二十多年过去了。一天，我在整理资料时，认真地查看了一张泛黄的旧报纸，「灯」立即跃进我的视线，这一盏「灯」我是那么熟悉、那么亲切，又保持得那么完整无损。我下意识地反应过来，我仍然要捡起笔，点燃心中的那盏「灯」，继续努力耕耘，来充实自己的快乐。

时代在发展，科技在进步，现在我的作文在网路上就能及时查看到，打印和装入信封邮寄的时代，已经成为历史翻过去的一页了。如今我又竖起了笔，写下了「换房前奏曲」、「一挂朝珠」、「峰记瓷号」等文章，而且编辑部事先还会通知我采用还是不能留用。

「灯」亮的时候，我心中有亮光、手上有力量、脚下有方向，它要我沿著灯光的闪亮继续前行！

## The "Lamp" Lights Up Again
Over twenty years ago, I came to the United States to join my family. After opening my suitcases and distributing the local Shanghai specialties I had brought to my daughter, son-in-law, and my daughter's in-laws, the initial excitement kept me thrilled for days. Over the following month, my daughter took me to visit major cities like San Francisco and Los Angeles. Afterward, she told me that they were very busy with work and couldn't accompany me shopping or chatting every day. She mentioned there was a large park near our home and suggested I go for a walk there every morning to find some friends to chat with.

Coming from the familiar, bustling city of Shanghai to live in San Diego in the American West, I found the surrounding environment, neighborhood, streets, and shops vastly different. Although the environment was beautiful and the neighborhood was landscaped like a park, there were no pedestrians on the streets, nor was there a single shop along either side. To buy things, one had to drive to the city center. For a while, I really struggled to adapt, thinking I would just have to adjust slowly and gradually get used to it.

One day, I went to the park. It was huge, but I barely saw anyone. There were only two fair-skinned, blue-eyed foreigners strolling with their little dogs, and my English proficiency was not good enough to communicate with them. Therefore, after my walk, I just sat blankly on a bench by myself, looking around aimlessly, searching for something.

Suddenly, a couple in their fifties approached me. "Are you Chinese?" the lady asked gently in greeting. With a broad smile, I replied, "Yes." Hearing my reply, she immediately switched to non-standard Beijing dialect and said, "That's wonderful! We are from Taiwan. My husband's surname is Tang." I immediately greeted them, "Mr. Tang, Mrs. Tang, I am so happy to meet Chinese friends today!"

When they learned that I was struggling with loneliness, they came up with a great idea. Mrs. Tang would bring me a copy of the previous day's "World Journal" every day. She said somewhat apologetically, "We read the newspaper first and give it to you the next day. Although it's yesterday's news, it will help you understand major events happening in the world, the situation in the US, and various literary and artistic news, which might help ease your boredom."

This made me overjoyed. With the newspaper keeping me company, my spirits suddenly lifted. I had mental nourishment, joy was added to my life, and a "lamp" lit up in my heart.

One day, as I was reading various articles in the "Homeland" section, I saw a warm prompt next to them: "As long as you pick up a pen, you are a writer." Reading this, I felt a sense of encouragement. I wondered if I also wanted to try being a writer. So, I rallied my courage, thought for a while, and picked up a pen to write my first short essay, titled "The Lamp."

That was back in 2000. At that time, we didn't use computers, there was no internet, and no email; everything was done manually. To ensure the characters were clear, I used a printer to print the words one by one. Then I read and checked it repeatedly, reviewing every word, sentence, and even punctuation mark several times. When I felt it looked like a proper essay and had some substance, I boldly put it in an envelope and mailed it to the World Journal's Homeland section.

Every day, I looked forward to the next-day newspaper sent by Mrs. Tang, hoping to see "The Lamp" appear in the Homeland section. I was too embarrassed to tell Mrs. Tang about my submission; if the essay was not accepted, I would be very embarrassed.

I waited anxiously, looking forward to it day after day. One day, Mrs. Tang said to me, "Mr. Tang's job has changed. He is being transferred to the company's headquarters in Los Angeles, and I will be moving with him."

When Mrs. Tang and I said goodbye, we were both very reluctant to part. I thanked her for her care, but in my heart, I felt helpless. Not only would I no longer see the good friend who delivered the newspaper to me every day, but I also lost contact with the World Journal. Did the Homeland section approve of "The Lamp"? Was it accepted? I had no way of knowing. At that time, some emotions arose in my mind: should I let the "lamp" in my heart continue to illuminate my world, or let it go out just like that?

After some time, I met my daughter's mother-in-law and told her about the situation. She was a warm-hearted and straightforward person. She mentioned that a friend of hers subscribed to the World Journal and received the newspaper daily, so she asked her friend to search for "The Lamp" in the Homeland section. This friend searched through the newspapers from over a month ago and finally found it. They quickly handed the newspaper to my daughter's mother-in-law, who then hurriedly mailed it to me from San Francisco. It took a lot of effort and trouble.

With the help of several people, I finally saw the essay "The Lamp" published in the Homeland section on September 30, 2000. You can imagine how excited I was back then! I was almost dancing with joy. The World Journal not only brought me happiness but also provided me with a platform to tell my stories.

I had a deep emotional connection with "The Lamp." Afterward, I tried writing a few more essays, such as "Flowers," "Longevity," and "Both Mentor and Friend," all of which were accepted and published by the newspaper. However, I never knew when they were published or what they looked like in print. I only knew a piece had been accepted when I received the manuscript fee, while others had not.

Springs came and autumns went; in the blink of an eye, over twenty years had passed. One day, while sorting through my files, I carefully looked at a yellowed old newspaper. "The Lamp" immediately caught my eye. This "lamp" was so familiar and dear to me, and it had been preserved so perfectly. Subconsciously, I reacted: I still want to pick up my pen, light up that "lamp" in my heart, and continue to work hard to enrich my own happiness.

Times are developing and technology is advancing. Now, my essays can be viewed online in real time. The era of printing and mailing envelopes has become a page turned in history. Today, I have picked up my pen again and written articles such as "Prelude to Changing Houses," "A String of Court Beads," and "Feng Kee Porcelain Shop." Furthermore, the editorial department now notifies me in advance whether my work will be accepted or not.

When the "lamp" is lit, I have light in my heart, strength in my hands, and direction under my feet. It guides me to keep moving forward along its shining light!
