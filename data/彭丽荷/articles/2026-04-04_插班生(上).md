任山梅的父母是道地的山东人，没有什么文化，只有做面食的技能，来到上海糊口养家，只得摆了一个大饼、油条摊。他们起早摸黑发面粉、生火，要抢在人们上班前把这些活都干好，然后做大饼、煎油条提供给需要的顾客，忙活一整天，勉强养活一家人。除了父母，任山梅有两个妹妹和一个小弟，一家六口就指望着这个大饼摊。

我每天上学都要经过任山梅的家门口，所以经常约她一同去学校。我们边走边聊天，她告诉我，下雨、刮风天，大饼油条的生意不好，一天没赚到几毛钱，生活不够开支，父母往往会为了一点小事就争吵，弟妹们都不敢作声，她也没有心思上课和专心做作业，虽然留了一级，仍然感到压力太大，心中很难过、害怕。

当班主任知道任山梅的情况后，就向我布置了任务：负责帮助任山梅学习上的困难，并希望放学后让我跟她一起完成家庭作业。她很会做家务，房子虽小，却收拾得干干净净、井井有条，还烧了一大锅泡饭，分别勺到四个碗中，中间放了两个菜：一小碟辣椒酱和一块红色乳腐，弟妹们围在一起吃饭。

突然，一个妹妹向弟弟喊道：「你怎么可以用两根筷子夹乳腐呢？」我听了正疑惑，任山梅不好意思地说：「辣椒酱是一分钱买的，红乳腐要两分，妈妈只允许在夹乳腐时用一根筷子蘸着就饭，否则吃完了，下顿就没了。」我真没想到贫穷会给生活带来如此的可怜和可怕。每逢过年过节，大饼油条也卖不动。在这样的日子里，任山梅的母亲也会想尽办法准备一点好吃的食物，分到每个盘子里，只有很小的几块肉，一条鱼放在中央是不可以动筷子的。

我确实是幸运的，初一起床，就看到了一个红包，里面有一元票面的两张压岁钱，我把它紧紧攥在手中，高兴了一阵子，然后妥妥地放在内衣的一个口袋中，并用一个别针把袋口牢牢封住。

开学了，我又约任山梅一起去学校，得知她没有任何压岁钱，毫不犹豫地从内衣袋里拿出了一元，塞进任山梅的手中，口中说道：「我俩平均分配，共同享受。」任山梅无论如何都不肯接受，我却说：「那么，我们就不是好朋友啦。」她才勉强收下。我俩虽然都是朦胧的少女，但是年少的情感总是纯洁无暇、真挚诚恳的。

在以后的日子里，我们一起做作业、复习功课、玩耍、聊天，任山梅的成绩慢慢地提高，跟上了班级，一次期末考试分数都拿到了A，她脸上的笑容也增多了不少。她为了感谢我，偶尔会带一根用油纸包好的老油条，塞进我的书包，有时也有半个烘焦的大饼。我知道这是她不舍得吃的珍贵食品，特意招待我的。

一次我不小心，右脚踝歪了，行走困难，任山梅每天都早早地来到我家门口，帮我背上书包，牵着我，让我靠在她的肩膀上慢步前行，一直到我完全恢复。我们年龄虽还小，但是已经懂得了知恩图报的道理，友情刻在了心中。（上）

## English Translation
The New Transfer Student (Part 1)

By Peng Lihe

At a church school, the students of Class 1, Grade 3 were listening intently to their homeroom teacher's Chinese lesson when the classroom door suddenly opened. A teacher from the dean's office walked in, leading a young girl. The teacher gave the homeroom teacher a knowing look, turned to pat the girl's shoulder, and told her, 'This is your class, your classmates, and your teacher.' In a warm tone, the homeroom teacher introduced her to everyone: 'This student is a new friend joining our class. From now on, she is a member of Class 1, Grade 3. Let's all give her a warm round of applause.'

The girl was shy and timid, her face turning bright red. In a faltering, trembling voice, she stuttered, 'My... my name is Ren Shanmei. I was a fourth-grade student at XX Elementary School in Shandong. My parents were worried that if I came to Shanghai to study, I wouldn't be able to keep up, so they had me go back to third grade. I hope you won't laugh at me.'

Ren Shanmei’s parents were native Shandong people with very little education. Their only skill was making wheat flour foods. To make a living in Shanghai, they set up a stall selling flatbread (dabing) and deep-fried dough sticks (youtiao). Working from dawn to dusk, they kneaded dough and lit the fire early in the morning, rushing to finish before people headed to work. They spent the entire day making flatbreads and frying dough sticks for their customers, barely managing to support the family. Besides her parents, Ren Shanmei had two younger sisters and a little brother; the family of six relied entirely on this small flatbread stall.

I had to pass by Ren Shanmei’s home on my way to school every day, so I often asked her to walk with me. As we walked and chatted, she told me that on rainy or windy days, business at the flatbread and dough stick stall would suffer, sometimes earning less than a few dimes a day. Since this wasn't enough to cover their living expenses, her parents would often argue over trivial matters. Her younger siblings didn't dare make a sound, and she couldn't focus on her classes or homework. Even though she had repeated a grade, she still felt immense pressure, making her feel sad and frightened.

When the homeroom teacher learned about Ren Shanmei's situation, she assigned me a task: to help Ren Shanmei with her academic difficulties and study with her to complete our homework after school. Ren Shanmei was very good at housework; although their house was small, it was kept spotlessly clean and orderly. She would cook a large pot of watered rice (paofan), scoop it into four bowls, and place two side dishes in the center: a small dish of chili paste and a piece of red fermented bean curd. Her younger siblings would gather around to eat.

Suddenly, one of the sisters shouted at the little brother, 'How can you use two chopsticks to pick up the fermented bean curd?' Hearing this, I was puzzled. Ren Shanmei said embarrassedly, 'The chili paste cost one cent, but the red fermented bean curd cost two cents. Mother only allows us to dip a single chopstick into the bean curd to flavor our rice; otherwise, if we eat it all, there won't be any left for the next meal.' I had never realized that poverty could bring such misery and fear to daily life. Even during holidays, the flatbreads and dough sticks wouldn't sell. On those days, Ren Shanmei's mother would still try her best to prepare some nice food, dividing it into small plates with just a few tiny pieces of meat. A whole fish would be placed in the center, but no one was allowed to touch it.

I was indeed lucky. On the morning of Lunar New Year's Day, I woke up to find a red envelope containing two one-yuan bills of New Year's money. Clutching it tightly in my hand, I rejoiced for a while before carefully placing it in an inner pocket of my underwear, securing the opening tightly with a safety pin.

When the new term began, I met up with Ren Shanmei to go to school again. Learning that she hadn't received any New Year's money, I didn't hesitate to pull out one yuan from my inner pocket, stuffed it into her hand, and said, 'We'll split it equally and enjoy it together.' Ren Shanmei refused to accept it no matter what, but I said, 'Well, if you don't, then we aren't good friends anymore.' Only then did she reluctantly accept it. Although we were both just innocent young girls, youth's emotions are always pure, flawless, sincere, and heartfelt.

In the days that followed, we did homework, reviewed lessons, played, and chatted together. Ren Shanmei's grades gradually improved, and she caught up with the class. In one final exam, she received straight A's, and the smile on her face returned much more often. To show her gratitude, she would occasionally slip a leftover dough stick wrapped in oil paper or half a toasted flatbread into my schoolbag. I knew these were precious foods she couldn't bear to eat herself, saved especially to treat me.

Once, I accidentally sprained my right ankle and had difficulty walking. Every day, Ren Shanmei would arrive at my doorstep early, help me carry my schoolbag, hold my hand, and let me lean on her shoulder as we walked slowly to school, continuing until I was fully recovered. Although we were still young, we already understood the meaning of returning a favor with gratitude, and our friendship was deeply etched into our hearts. (Part 1)
