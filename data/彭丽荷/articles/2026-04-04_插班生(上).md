# 插班生(上)

**Date:** 2026-04-04 02:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷
**URL:** https://www.worldjournal.com/wj/story/121251/9410798?zh-cn

在一所教会学校，三年一班的学生都聚精会神地专心聆听班主任老师讲语文课时，突然教室的门被打开了，是教导处的一名老师带了一个小女孩走进教室，这名老师向班主任递了个眼神，转过身用手拍了拍女孩的肩膀，告诉女孩：「这就是妳的班级、妳的同学和老师。」班主任用温和的语气向大家介绍：「这名同学是来到我们班级的新朋友，从现在起她就是我们三年一班的成员，我们大家鼓掌欢迎她。」

女孩腼腆、胆怯，满脸涨得通红，用走调的声音结结巴巴地说：「我叫任山梅，是山东XX小学四年级的学生，爸爸妈妈说怕我到上海读书，跟不上，叫我上三年级，希望同学们莫要笑话我。」

任山梅的父母是道地的山东人，没有什么文化，只有做面食的技能，来到上海糊口养家，只得摆了一个大饼、油条摊。他们起早摸黑发面粉、生火，要抢在人们上班前把这些活都干好，然后做大饼、煎油条提供给需要的顾客，忙活一整天，勉强养活一家人。除了父母，任山梅有两个妹妹和一个小弟，一家六口就指望着这个大饼摊。

我每天上学都要经过任山梅的家门口，所以经常约她一同去学校。我们边走边聊天，她告诉我，下雨、刮风天，大饼油条的生意不好，一天没赚到几毛钱，生活不够开支，父母往往会为了一点小事就争吵，弟妹们都不敢作声，她也没有心思上课和专心做作业，虽然留了一级，仍然感到压力太大，心中很难过、害怕。

当班主任知道任山梅的情况后，就向我布置了任务：负责帮助任山梅学习上的困难，并希望放学后让我跟她一起完成家庭作业。她很会做家务，房子虽小，却收拾得干干净净、井井有条，还烧了一大锅泡饭，分别勺到四个碗中，中间放了两个菜：一小碟辣椒酱和一块红色乳腐，弟妹们围在一起吃饭。

突然，一个妹妹向弟弟喊道：「你怎么可以用两根筷子夹乳腐呢？」我听了正疑惑，任山梅不好意思地说：「辣椒酱是一分钱买的，红乳腐要两分，妈妈只允许在夹乳腐时用一根筷子蘸着就饭，否则吃完了，下顿就没了。」我真没想到贫穷会给生活带来如此的可怜和可怕。每逢过年过节，大饼油条也卖不动。在这样的日子里，任山梅的母亲也会想尽办法准备一点好吃的食物，分到每个盘子里，只有很小的几块肉，一条鱼放在中央是不可以动筷子的。

我确实是幸运的，初一起床，就看到了一个红包，里面有一元票面的两张压岁钱，我把它紧紧攥在手中，高兴了一阵子，然后妥妥地放在内衣的一个口袋中，并用一个别针把袋口牢牢封住。

开学了，我又约任山梅一起去学校，得知她没有任何压岁钱，毫不犹豫地从内衣袋里拿出了一元，塞进任山梅的手中，口中说道：「我俩平均分配，共同享受。」任山梅无论如何都不肯接受，我却说：「那么，我们就不是好朋友啦。」她才勉强收下。我俩虽然都是朦胧的少女，但是年少的情感总是纯洁无暇、真挚诚恳的。

在以后的日子里，我们一起做作业、复习功课、玩耍、聊天，任山梅的成绩慢慢地提高，跟上了班级，一次期末考试分数都拿到了A，她脸上的笑容也增多了不少。她为了感谢我，偶尔会带一根用油纸包好的老油条，塞进我的书包，有时也有半个烘焦的大饼。我知道这是她不舍得吃的珍贵食品，特意招待我的。

一次我不小心，右脚踝歪了，行走困难，任山梅每天都早早地来到我家门口，帮我背上书包，牵着我，让我靠在她的肩膀上慢步前行，一直到我完全恢复。我们年龄虽还小，但是已经懂得了知恩图报的道理，友情刻在了心中。（上）

## The Transfer Student (Part 1)
In a missionary school, while the students of Class 1, Grade 3 were listening intently to their homeroom teacher's Chinese lesson, the classroom door was suddenly opened. A teacher from the dean's office brought in a little girl, gave the homeroom teacher a meaningful look, turned around, patted the girl's shoulder, and told her, "This is your classroom, your classmates, and your teacher." The homeroom teacher introduced her to everyone in a warm voice: "This classmate is a new friend joining our class. From now on, she is a member of Class 1, Grade 3. Let's all applaud to welcome her."

The girl was shy and timid, her face flushed red. In a faltering, out-of-tune voice, she stammered, "My name is Ren Shanmei. I was a fourth-grade student at XX Primary School in Shandong. My parents were worried that I wouldn't be able to keep up with the schoolwork here in Shanghai, so they had me enroll in the third grade. I hope my classmates won't laugh at me."

Ren Shanmei's parents were typical Shandong natives with little education. They only knew how to make wheaten food, so they set up a flatbread and fried dough stick stall to make a living in Shanghai. They woke up early and worked late, kneading flour and lighting the fire, rushing to get everything ready before people went to work, then spent the whole day making flatbreads and frying dough sticks for their customers, barely managing to support the family. Besides her parents, Ren Shanmei had two younger sisters and a younger brother; the family of six relied entirely on this flatbread stall.

Every day on my way to school, I would pass by Ren Shanmei's house, so I often asked her to walk with me. We would chat as we walked. She told me that on rainy and windy days, the flatbread and fried dough stick business was bad, and they barely earned a few cents a day, which wasn't enough to cover their living expenses. Her parents would often argue over trivial matters, and her younger siblings didn't dare make a sound. She also couldn't focus in class or on her homework. Even though she had held back a grade, she still felt immense pressure and felt very sad and afraid.

When our homeroom teacher learned about Ren Shanmei's situation, she assigned me a task: to help Ren Shanmei with her learning difficulties and study with her after school to complete our homework. Ren Shanmei was very good at housework; although her home was small, she kept it clean and orderly. She would cook a large pot of watery rice, scoop it into four bowls, and place two dishes in the middle: a small saucer of chili paste and a piece of red fermented bean curd. Her siblings would gather around to eat.

Suddenly, one sister shouted at their brother, "How can you use two chopsticks to pick up the fermented bean curd?" Hearing this, I was puzzled. Ren Shanmei said embarrassedly, "The chili paste cost one cent, and the red fermented bean curd cost two cents. My mother only allows us to dip a single chopstick into the bean curd to flavor our rice; otherwise, if we eat it all, there won't be any for the next meal." I had never imagined that poverty could bring such misery and fear to life. Even during New Year and other holidays, flatbreads and fried dough sticks wouldn't sell well. On those days, Ren Shanmei's mother would try her best to prepare a little nice food, distributing it into each plate with only a few tiny pieces of meat, and a fish placed in the center was strictly off-limits.

I was indeed fortunate. On the morning of Lunar New Year's Day, as soon as I got out of bed, I saw a red envelope containing two one-yuan bills of New Year's money. I gripped it tightly in my hand, rejoiced for a while, and then placed it safely in an inner pocket of my underwear, pinning the opening securely with a safety pin.

When the new semester started, I asked Ren Shanmei to go to school together again and learned that she hadn't received any New Year's money. Without hesitation, I took out one yuan from my inner pocket, stuffed it into her hand, and said, "We'll split it equally and enjoy it together." Ren Shanmei refused to accept it no matter what, but I said, "Well, then we aren't good friends anymore." Only then did she reluctantly accept it. Although we were both just naive young girls, our youthful feelings were pure, innocent, and sincere.

In the days that followed, we did homework, reviewed lessons, played, and chatted together. Ren Shanmei's grades gradually improved, and she caught up with the class. She even got A's in all her subjects during a final exam, and the smile on her face grew. To show her gratitude, she would occasionally tuck a cold fried dough stick wrapped in oil paper into my schoolbag, or sometimes half a toasted flatbread. I knew these were precious foods she couldn't bear to eat herself, specially saved to treat me.

Once, I accidentally sprained my right ankle and had difficulty walking. Ren Shanmei came to my house early every morning, helped carry my schoolbag, held my hand, and let me lean on her shoulder as we walked slowly to school until I fully recovered. Though we were still young, we already understood the principle of returning kindness, and our friendship was deeply etched into our hearts. (Part 1)
