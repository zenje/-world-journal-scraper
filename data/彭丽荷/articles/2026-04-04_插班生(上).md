# 插班生(上)

**Date:** 2026-04-04 02:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷

在一所教会学校，三年一班的学生都聚精会神地专心聆听班主任老师讲语文课时，突然教室的门被打开了，是教导处的一名老师带了一个小女孩走进教室，这名老师向班主任递了个眼神，转过身用手拍了拍女孩的肩膀，告诉女孩：「这就是妳的班级、妳的同学和老师。」班主任用温和的语气向大家介绍：「这名同学是来到我们班级的新朋友，从现在起她就是我们三年一班的成员，我们大家鼓掌欢迎她。」

女孩腼腆、胆怯，满脸涨得通红，用走调的声音结结巴巴地说：「我叫任山梅，是山东XX小学四年级的学生，爸爸妈妈说怕我到上海读书，跟不上，叫我上三年级，希望同学们莫要笑话我。」

任山梅的父母是道地的山东人，没有什么文化，只有做面食的技能，来到上海糊口养家，只得摆了一个大饼、油条摊。他们起早摸黑发面粉、生火，要抢在人们上班前把这些活都干好，然后做大饼、煎油条提供给需要的顾客，忙活一整天，勉强养活一家人。除了父母，任山梅有两个妹妹和一个小弟，一家六口就指望着这个大饼摊。

我每天上学都要经过任山梅的家门口，所以经常约她一同去学校。我们边走边聊天，她告诉我，下雨、刮风天，大饼油条的生意不好，一天没赚到几毛钱，生活不够开支，父母往往会为了一点小事就争吵，弟妹们都不敢作声，她也没有心思上课和专心做作业，虽然留了一级，仍然感到压力太大，心中很难过、害怕。

当班主任知道任山梅的情况后，就向我布置了任务：负责帮助任山梅学习上的困难，并希望放学后让我跟她一起完成家庭作业。她很会做家务，房子虽小，却收拾得干干净净、井井有条，还烧了一大锅泡饭，分别勺到四个碗中，中间放了两个菜：一小碟辣椒酱和一块红色乳腐，弟妹们围在一起吃饭。

突然，一个妹妹向弟弟喊道：「你怎么可以用两根筷子夹乳腐呢？」我听了正疑惑，任山梅不好意思地说：「辣椒酱是一分钱买的，红乳腐要两分，妈妈只允许在夹乳腐时用一根筷子蘸着就饭，否则吃完了，下顿就没了。」我真没想到贫穷会给生活带来如此的可怜和可怕。每逢过年过节，大饼油条也卖不动。在这样的日子里，任山梅的母亲也会想尽办法准备一点好吃的食物，分到每个盘子里，只有很小的几块肉，一条鱼放在中央是不可以动筷子的。

我确实是幸运的，初一起床，就看到了一个红包，里面有一元票面的两张压岁钱，我把它紧紧攥在手中，高兴了一阵子，然后妥妥地放在内衣的一个口袋中，并用一个别针把袋口牢牢封住。

开学了，我又约任山梅一起去学校，得知她没有任何压岁钱，毫不犹豫地从内衣袋里拿出了一元，塞进任山梅的手中，口中说道：「我俩平均分配，共同享受。」任山梅无论如何都不肯接受，我却说：「那么，我们就不是好朋友啦。」她才勉强收下。我俩虽然都是朦胧的少女，但是年少的情感总是纯洁无暇、真挚诚恳的。

在以后的日子里，我们一起做作业、复习功课、玩耍、聊天，任山梅的成绩慢慢地提高，跟上了班级，一次期末考试分数都拿到了A，她脸上的笑容也增多了不少。她为了感谢我，偶尔会带一根用油纸包好的老油条，塞进我的书包，有时也有半个烘焦的大饼。我知道这是她不舍得吃的珍贵食品，特意招待我的。

一次我不小心，右脚踝歪了，行走困难，任山梅每天都早早地来到我家门口，帮我背上书包，牵着我，让我靠在她的肩膀上慢步前行，一直到我完全恢复。我们年龄虽还小，但是已经懂得了知恩图报的道理，友情刻在了心中。（上）

## English Translation
# The Transfer Student (Part 1)

**Date:** 2026-04-04 02:00 ET  
**Author:** Peng Lihe  

In a church school, while the students of Class 1, Grade 3 were focusing all their attention on their homeroom teacher's Chinese lesson, the classroom door suddenly opened. A teacher from the dean's office led a young girl into the room. The teacher gave the homeroom teacher a quick look, turned to pat the girl on her shoulder, and told her, "This is your class, your classmates, and your teacher." In a gentle voice, the homeroom teacher introduced her to everyone: "This student is a new friend joining our class. From now on, she is a member of Class 1, Grade 3. Let's all applaud and welcome her."

Shy and timid, her face flushed bright red, the girl stammered in an out-of-tune voice, "My name is Ren Shanmei. I was a fourth-grade student at XX Primary School in Shandong. My parents were afraid that if I went to school in Shanghai, I wouldn't be able to keep up, so they had me enroll in the third grade. I hope my classmates won't laugh at me."

Ren Shanmei's parents were authentic Shandong natives with little education. Possessing only the skill of making wheat-based foods, they had come to Shanghai to make a living and were forced to set up a stall selling flatbreads and deep-fried dough sticks (youtiao). They rose early and worked late into the night, proofing flour and lighting the fire, rushing to get all the prep work done before people went to work. Then they would make flatbreads and fry dough sticks for customers, busying themselves all day long just to barely feed their family. Besides her parents, Ren Shanmei had two younger sisters and a little brother; the entire family of six relied solely on this flatbread stall.

Every day on my way to school, I had to pass by the door of Ren Shanmei's home, so I often asked her to walk to school with me. As we walked and talked, she told me that on rainy and windy days, business at the flatbread and dough stick stall was poor. They wouldn't even earn a few dimes a day, which wasn't enough to cover living expenses. Her parents would often bicker over trivial matters, leaving her younger siblings too frightened to make a sound. She, too, couldn't focus on her classes or homework. Even though she had repeated a grade, she still felt immense pressure, feeling deeply sad and afraid.

When our homeroom teacher learned about Ren Shanmei's situation, she assigned me a task: to help Ren Shanmei with her academic difficulties and to work on our homework together after school. She was very good at housework. Although their home was small, she kept it neat, tidy, and orderly. She had also cooked a large pot of paofan (rice soaked in broth), ladled it into four bowls, and placed two dishes in the middle: a small saucer of chili paste and a piece of red fermented bean curd (furu). The younger siblings gathered around to eat.

Suddenly, one of her sisters yelled at her brother, "How can you use two chopsticks to pick up the fermented bean curd?" Hearing this, I was puzzled. Ren Shanmei explained sheepishly, "The chili paste cost one cent, but the red fermented bean curd cost two cents. Mom only allows us to dip a single chopstick into the bean curd to flavor our rice; otherwise, if we eat it all, there won't be any left for the next meal." I never imagined that poverty could bring such pitiful and frightening constraints to life. During festivals and New Year holidays, flatbreads and dough sticks wouldn't sell either. On those days, Ren Shanmei's mother would try her best to prepare some tasty food, portioning it onto each plate. There were only a few tiny pieces of meat, and a single fish placed in the center was strictly off-limits to anyone's chopsticks.

I was indeed fortunate. On the morning of Lunar New Year's Day, as soon as I got out of bed, I saw a red envelope containing two one-yuan bills of New Year's money. I clutched it tightly in my hand, overjoyed for a long while, before securely placing it in an inner pocket of my underwear and pinning the opening shut with a safety pin.

When the new semester began, I met up with Ren Shanmei to go to school again. Learning that she hadn't received any New Year's money, I didn't hesitate to pull out one yuan from my inner pocket, stuffed it into her hand, and said, "Let's split it evenly and enjoy it together." Ren Shanmei refused to accept it no matter what, but I said, "Well, then we aren't best friends anymore." Only then did she reluctantly accept it. Although we were both just naive young girls, our youthful feelings were pure, flawless, sincere, and earnest.

In the days that followed, we did homework, reviewed lessons, played, and chatted together. Ren Shanmei's grades gradually improved until she caught up with the class, even scoring straight A's on a final exam. The smiles on her face grew more frequent. To show her gratitude, she would occasionally slip a leftover dough stick wrapped in oil paper into my schoolbag, or sometimes half of a toasted flatbread. I knew these were precious foods she couldn't bear to eat herself, specially saved to treat me.

Once, I accidentally twisted my right ankle, making it difficult to walk. Ren Shanmei would come to my door early every morning, help carry my schoolbag, hold my hand, and let me lean on her shoulder as we walked slowly, continuing this until I was fully recovered. Although we were still young, we already understood the principle of returning kindness with gratitude, and our friendship was deeply etched into our hearts. (Part 1)
