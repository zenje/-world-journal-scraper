**Date:** 2026-04-05 02:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷

时间飞快，我们已经走完第三个春秋，升到六年级了。我和任山梅约定毕业以后去考同一所中学，我们的理想也差不多，将来做一个为国家有用的人材，我们两人不离不散做永久的好朋友。

毕业前两个月的一天早晨，我仍然去约她一同上学，只见她红着眼圈，看着我，想开口说话，声音没有发出来，泪水哗啦啦地直流。我急忙问她发生了什么事，她用嘶哑低沉的嗓音告诉我，她要离开学校了，不能再和我天天一同去上学；妈妈要她去一个袜厂当学徒、做工，说是为她找到了出路，还能赚钱帮助一下家庭。这是一个老乡为她争取到的机会，叫她好好想想，一切都是为她个人和家庭考虑的。

她又说，第一她舍不得学校，第二不愿意离开我，可是现实又不能违背，心里乱得慌。我一听，轰的一声，怎么会这样啊？万万没有料到小小年纪，她才十四岁，就叫她去干活打工，这不是童工吗？国家是不允许的。我的眼圈也湿润了。任山梅说，妈妈谎报了她的年龄，关照不要声张，否则名额就被别人抢走。无奈，困难的家庭扭不过现实生活，她哪有力量挽救这个局面呢，只能屈服。我哑口无言，只有难过和失望，呆呆地站在那里很久很久。

任山梅去袜厂做工，工厂基本都是年轻的女工，她们工作辛苦，三班倒，轮流操作生产机器，整整八个小时都是站着的活；思想要高度集中，织袜的过程中，跳了针就是次品，同时还要受到工头的严厉训斥。

由于三班倒的缘故，任山梅搬到工厂的女工宿舍去住了，打那以后，我和她基本上见不到面，我每天上学路过她家门口时，总会不自禁地在那儿站一会，有一股失落感笼罩着我。后来，我们的共同言语也不在一个层次上，逐渐联系少了，疏远了，淡忘了。

文化大革命，开始是打倒资产阶级、反动学术权威，第二步是敢于摸老虎屁股，接着各部门、各单位的当权派都靠边站，成立了革命委员会，后来形成了保皇派和造反派，两大派系互相攻击、争斗，两派无休止地争吵。

此时，工宣队进驻上层建筑，如高等院校、设计院、研究所等单位，纲领是「工人阶级必须领导一切，向工人同志学习，接受工人阶级的再教育」。我在研究所工作快一年了，这样的大风大浪没有经历过，搞不清什么派什么派的，当了个逍遥派，倒也挺自在。

一天去单位上班，只见办公室的大门敞开，每个同事包括我的办公桌子抽屉全粘贴了封条，然后一个工宣队员，叫我们把自己的名字写在台子上，以便对得上号，方便核查。我把自己的名字写在桌面上，其他的同事也都一样。工宣队命令写好的人员都离开现场，他们要检查每个人抽屉里是否有「封资修」的东西。

我的抽屉里放有几张电影院做广告的外国明星照片，照片上是穿戴时髦、袒胸露背、性感十足的美女和模特，我一时很紧张害怕，深怕给我戴个什么帽子。检查完毕，我回到原座位上，赶忙拉开了抽屉，一看，没有丝毫翻动过，还是老样子，照片仍旧躺在抽屉的底层。我疑惑不解。

到了中午电话响了，传话叫我到工宣队办公室去，我心里像打鼓似地焦虑、紧张、害怕，腿也发软，不知会发生什么？办公室只有一名女同志，而且是工宣队的大队长，她叫了我一声，说：「请坐。」我坐在她的对面，低着头不敢正视她。

「我叫任山梅，妳不认识我了吗？」我擡起头，又惊又喜，又激动又兴奋：「啊！老同学、老朋友，怎么是妳？」

研究所里带我工作的一个老师，他在工作和生活方面总照顾关心着我，而且要重点培养我，现在正关在牛棚里劳动改造，被定性为「反动学术权威」。我对任山梅说：「只求妳一件事情，在不为难妳的情况下，能否帮助这个老师解放出牛棚，脱掉帽子，让他恢复正常的生活和工作？拜托了。」

一个星期以后，老师回到了办公室，高兴地向组员们说：「感谢工宣队，把我的事情调查清楚了，让我和大家继续努力工作。」

## English Translation
The Transfer Student (Part 2)

Date: April 5, 2026, 2:00 AM ET
Author: Peng Lihe

Time flew by. We had already completed three autumns and winters together and had entered the sixth grade. Ren Shanmei and I made a pact to take the entrance exam for the same middle school after graduation. Our aspirations were also very similar: to become useful citizens for our country in the future, and to remain inseparable, lifelong best friends.

One morning, two months before graduation, I went to meet her to walk to school together as usual. I saw her with red, puffy eyes, looking at me. She wanted to say something, but before any sound could come out, her tears began to stream down. I hurriedly asked her what had happened. In a raspy, low voice, she told me that she was leaving school and would no longer be able to walk with me every day. Her mother wanted her to go to a hosiery factory to work as an apprentice. Her mother said they had found a path forward for her, and she could earn money to help the family. A fellow villager had secured this opportunity for her, telling her to think it over carefully, and that everything was being done with her personal and family interests in mind.

She added that, first, she couldn't bear to leave school, and second, she didn't want to leave me, but she couldn't defy reality, and her heart was in absolute turmoil. Hearing this, my head buzzed. How could this happen? I had never expected that at such a young age—she was only fourteen—she would be sent to work. Wasn't that child labor? The state did not allow it. My eyes welled up with tears too. Ren Shanmei said her mother had lied about her age and warned her not to say a word, otherwise someone else would snatch the spot. Helpless, a family in hardship could not overcome the harsh realities of life. What power did she have to save herself from this situation? She could only submit. I was left speechless, feeling nothing but grief and disappointment, standing there in a daze for a long, long time.

Ren Shanmei went to work at the hosiery factory. The factory was mostly staffed by young female workers who worked grueling, three-shift rotations. They took turns operating the machinery, standing for a full eight hours. Their minds had to be highly focused; in the process of weaving socks, a dropped stitch meant a defective product, which would also invite severe reprimands from the foreman.

Because of the three-shift system, Ren Shanmei moved into the female workers' dormitory at the factory. After that, we hardly ever saw each other. Every day on my way to school, when I passed her doorstep, I would involuntarily stand there for a moment, enveloped by a sense of loss. Later on, our shared language was no longer on the same level; our communication gradually grew less frequent, we drifted apart, and we eventually faded from each other's memories.

During the Cultural Revolution, the first step was to overthrow the bourgeoisie and reactionary academic authorities. The second step was to dare to challenge those in power. Subsequently, the power-holders in all departments and units were sidelined, and Revolutionary Committees were established. Later, the "Royalists" and "Rebels" factions were formed, and the two major factions attacked, fought, and bickered with each other endlessly.

At this time, Worker Propaganda Teams entered the "superstructure," such as universities, design institutes, and research institutes. Their slogan was "The working class must lead everything, learn from our worker comrades, and accept re-education from the working class." I had been working at the research institute for nearly a year. Having never experienced such great winds and waves, and unable to make head or tail of the various factions, I became a bystander (unaligned), which was actually quite comfortable.

One day when I went to work, I saw the office doors wide open. The drawers of every colleague, including my own desk, were sealed with paper strips. Then, a member of the Worker Propaganda Team ordered us to write our names on our desks so they could match the names to the desks for easy verification. I wrote my name on the desktop, and my colleagues did the same. The Worker Propaganda Team ordered everyone who had finished writing to leave the premises, as they were going to search everyone's drawers for "feudal, capitalist, and revisionist" materials.

In my drawer, I kept several photos of foreign movie stars used for cinema advertisements. The photos featured fashionable, low-cut, highly sensual actresses and models. I became extremely nervous and terrified, deeply afraid that I would be labeled with some political offense. After the inspection was over, I returned to my seat and quickly pulled open the drawer. To my surprise, nothing had been disturbed; everything was just as it had been, with the photos still lying at the very bottom of the drawer. I was utterly bewildered.

At noon, the phone rang, summoning me to the Worker Propaganda Team's office. My heart was pounding like a drum with anxiety, tension, and fear. My legs felt weak, and I had no idea what was about to happen. There was only one female comrade in the office, who happened to be the head of the Worker Propaganda Team. She called out my name and said, "Please, have a seat." I sat opposite her, keeping my head bowed, not daring to look her in the eye.

"My name is Ren Shanmei. Don't you recognize me?" I lifted my head, filled with shock, joy, excitement, and emotion. "Ah! Old classmate, old friend, how is it you?"

A senior colleague who mentored me at the research institute always looked after and cared for me in both my work and personal life, and had intended to groom me as a key successor. He was currently confined to a "cowshed" (confinement for political targets) undergoing labor reform, having been classified as a "reactionary academic authority." I said to Ren Shanmei, "I only ask one thing of you. If it doesn't put you in a difficult position, could you help release this teacher from the cowshed, remove his political label, and allow him to return to his normal life and work? I beg of you."

A week later, the teacher returned to the office. He happily told the team members, "Thanks to the Worker Propaganda Team, they investigated my matters thoroughly and have allowed me to continue working hard alongside everyone."
