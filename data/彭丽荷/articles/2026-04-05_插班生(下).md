# 插班生(下)

**Date:** 2026-04-05 02:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷
**URL:** https://www.worldjournal.com/wj/story/121251/9411619?zh-cn

时间飞快，我们已经走完第三个春秋，升到六年级了。我和任山梅约定毕业以后去考同一所中学，我们的理想也差不多，将来做一个为国家有用的人材，我们两人不离不散做永久的好朋友。

毕业前两个月的一天早晨，我仍然去约她一同上学，只见她红着眼圈，看着我，想开口说话，声音没有发出来，泪水哗啦啦地直流。我急忙问她发生了什么事，她用嘶哑低沉的嗓音告诉我，她要离开学校了，不能再和我天天一同去上学；妈妈要她去一个袜厂当学徒、做工，说是为她找到了出路，还能赚钱帮助一下家庭。这是一个老乡为她争取到的机会，叫她好好想想，一切都是为她个人和家庭考虑的。

她又说，第一她舍不得学校，第二不愿意离开我，可是现实又不能违背，心里乱得慌。我一听，轰的一声，怎么会这样啊？万万没有料到小小年纪，她才十四岁，就叫她去干活打工，这不是童工吗？国家是不允许的。我的眼圈也湿润了。任山梅说，妈妈谎报了她的年龄，关照不要声张，否则名额就被别人抢走。无奈，困难的家庭扭不过现实生活，她哪有力量挽救这个局面呢，只能屈服。我哑口无言，只有难过和失望，呆呆地站在那里很久很久。

任山梅去袜厂做工，工厂基本都是年轻的女工，她们工作辛苦，三班倒，轮流操作生产机器，整整八个小时都是站着的活；思想要高度集中，织袜的过程中，跳了针就是次品，同时还要受到工头的严厉训斥。

由于三班倒的缘故，任山梅搬到工厂的女工宿舍去住了，打那以后，我和她基本上见不到面，我每天上学路过她家门口时，总会不自禁地在那儿站一会，有一股失落感笼罩着我。后来，我们的共同言语也不在一个层次上，逐渐联系少了，疏远了，淡忘了。

文化大革命，开始是打倒资产阶级、反动学术权威，第二步是敢于摸老虎屁股，接着各部门、各单位的当权派都靠边站，成立了革命委员会，后来形成了保皇派和造反派，两大派系互相攻击、争斗，两派无休止地争吵。

此时，工宣队进驻上层建筑，如高等院校、设计院、研究所等单位，纲领是「工人阶级必须领导一切，向工人同志学习，接受工人阶级的再教育」。我在研究所工作快一年了，这样的大风大浪没有经历过，搞不清什么派什么派的，当了个逍遥派，倒也挺自在。

一天去单位上班，只见办公室的大门敞开，每个同事包括我的办公桌子抽屉全粘贴了封条，然后一个工宣队员，叫我们把自己的名字写在台子上，以便对得上号，方便核查。我把自己的名字写在桌面上，其他的同事也都一样。工宣队命令写好的人员都离开现场，他们要检查每个人抽屉里是否有「封资修」的东西。

我的抽屉里放有几张电影院做广告的外国明星照片，照片上是穿戴时髦、袒胸露背、性感十足的美女和模特，我一时很紧张害怕，深怕给我戴个什么帽子。检查完毕，我回到原座位上，赶忙拉开了抽屉，一看，没有丝毫翻动过，还是老样子，照片仍旧躺在抽屉的底层。我疑惑不解。

到了中午电话响了，传话叫我到工宣队办公室去，我心里像打鼓似地焦虑、紧张、害怕，腿也发软，不知会发生什么？办公室只有一名女同志，而且是工宣队的大队长，她叫了我一声，说：「请坐。」我坐在她的对面，低着头不敢正视她。

「我叫任山梅，妳不认识我了吗？」我擡起头，又惊又喜，又激动又兴奋：「啊！老同学、老朋友，怎么是妳？」

研究所里带我工作的一个老师，他在工作和生活方面总照顾关心着我，而且要重点培养我，现在正关在牛棚里劳动改造，被定性为「反动学术权威」。我对任山梅说：「只求妳一件事情，在不为难妳的情况下，能否帮助这个老师解放出牛棚，脱掉帽子，让他恢复正常的生活和工作？拜托了。」

一个星期以后，老师回到了办公室，高兴地向组员们说：「感谢工宣队，把我的事情调查清楚了，让我和大家继续努力工作。」

## The Transfer Student (Part 2)
Time flew by. We had already completed our third year and entered the sixth grade. Ren Shanmei and I made a promise to take the entrance exam for the same middle school after graduation. Our ideals were also very similar—to become useful talents for the country in the future, and to remain inseparable, lifelong best friends.

One morning, two months before graduation, I went to meet her to walk to school together as usual. I saw her with red, swollen eyes, looking at me. She wanted to speak, but no sound came out, and her tears flowed uncontrollably. I hurriedly asked her what had happened. In a raspy, low voice, she told me that she was leaving school and could no longer go to school with me every day. Her mother wanted her to work as an apprentice in a sock factory, saying that they had found a way forward for her, and she could earn some money to help the family. This was an opportunity secured by a fellow villager, who told her to think it over carefully, saying it was all for her own good and for her family.

She also said that, first, she couldn't bear to leave school, and second, she didn't want to leave me. However, she could not defy reality, and her mind was in a chaotic mess. Hearing this, my mind went blank with a loud bang—how could this be? I never expected that at such a young age, just fourteen, she would be made to work. Wasn't that child labor? The country did not allow it. My eyes welled up too. Ren Shanmei said her mother had lied about her age and warned her not to tell anyone, otherwise the spot would be snatched up by someone else. Helplessly, a struggling family could not fight against the reality of life. What power did she have to change the situation? She could only submit. I was speechless, left only with sadness and disappointment, standing there dazed for a very long time.

Ren Shanmei went to work at the sock factory. The factory was mostly staffed by young female workers. Their work was exhausting, with three rotating shifts, taking turns to operate production machinery. They had to stand for a full eight hours. Their minds had to be highly focused; during the knitting process, a dropped stitch meant a defective product, and they would also face severe scolding from the foreman.

Due to the rotating shifts, Ren Shanmei moved into the factory's female dormitory. From then on, she and I rarely saw each other. Every day on my way to school, as I passed by her house, I couldn't help but stand there for a moment, enveloped by a sense of loss. Later on, we no longer shared common topics on the same level, our communication gradually decreased, we drifted apart, and we forgot each other.

The Cultural Revolution began with knocking down the bourgeoisie and reactionary academic authorities. The second step was daring to challenge authority. Next, the power holders in every department and unit were set aside, and Revolutionary Committees were established. Later, the Royalists and the Rebels were formed, and the two major factions attacked and fought each other, arguing endlessly.

At this time, Worker Propaganda Teams entered the superstructure, such as higher education institutions, design institutes, and research institutes, with the core slogan: 'The working class must lead everything, learn from worker comrades, and accept re-education from the working class.' I had been working at the research institute for nearly a year. Having never experienced such massive storms and waves, and unable to figure out who belonged to which faction, I became a 'by-stander,' which was actually quite comfortable.

One day, when I went to work, I saw that the office doors were wide open. The drawers of every colleague's desk, including mine, had been sealed with paper strips. Then, a member of the Worker Propaganda Team asked us to write our names on our desks to match our identities and facilitate verification. I wrote my name on the desk surface, and my colleagues did the same. The Worker Propaganda Team ordered everyone who had finished writing to leave the scene, as they wanted to inspect everyone's drawers to see if there was any 'feudal, capitalist, or revisionist' material inside.

I had placed a few photos of foreign movie stars used for cinema advertisements in my drawer. In the photos, there were stylish, low-cut, highly sexualized beautiful women and models. I was extremely nervous and terrified, deeply afraid of being labeled with some bad reputation. After the inspection was over, I returned to my seat and quickly pulled open the drawer. Looking inside, it hadn't been rummaged through at all; everything was exactly as it was, and the photos were still lying at the bottom of the drawer. I was utterly bewildered.

At noon, the phone rang, telling me to go to the Worker Propaganda Team's office. My heart beat like a drum, filled with anxiety, tension, and fear. My legs felt weak, and I didn't know what was going to happen. There was only one female comrade in the office, and she was the leader of the Worker Propaganda Team. She called out to me and said, 'Please sit down.' I sat opposite her, keeping my head down, not daring to look her in the eye.

'My name is Ren Shanmei. Don't you recognize me?' I raised my head, pleasantly surprised, excited, and thrilled: 'Ah! My old classmate, my old friend, how is it you?'

A mentor who trained me at the research institute always took care of and concerned himself with me in both work and life, and was focusing on cultivating my skills. Now, he was locked in a 'cowshed' undergoing reform through labor, categorized as a 'reactionary academic authority.' I said to Ren Shanmei, 'I only ask you for one thing. If it doesn't put you in a difficult position, could you help release this teacher from the cowshed, remove his label, and let him return to his normal life and work? Please.'

A week later, the teacher returned to the office. He happily told the team members, 'Thanks to the Worker Propaganda Team, they cleared up my situation and have let me continue working hard with everyone.'
