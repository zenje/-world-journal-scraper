# 阿米哥

**Date:** 2025-12-02 01:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷

「阿米哥（Amigo）」，是[**墨西哥**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/墨西哥)人对朋友的一种尊重而友好的称呼，初次见面叫一声「朋友」，心中就有了一股浓浓的暖意。真没料到，到美国三十多年，竟与一个名叫「班」的阿米哥成为好朋友。

三十多年前，女儿、女婿刚在美国站稳脚跟，他们在[**圣地牙哥**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/圣地牙哥)（San Diego）买了一幢独立的小房子，三房二卫。房子虽小，但是后花园倒挺大的，有高大的芭蕉树、杨柳树、椰子树、宽广的草坪，后面还有个小山坡。

秋天树叶泛黄，秋叶吹落在整个后院，枯萎的树枝也垂落挂在树干上，草坪上的野草、杂草和青草参差不齐地冒在阳光下，小山坡上也是杂草丛生、一片乱象。

女儿说，这种萎靡不振、杂乱糟糕的环境得赶快收拾一下。那时，他俩收入不多，家境不富裕，为了节省费用，只能自己动手打理这些粗活。有一天，女儿、女婿换上了破旧的劳动服，卷起了袖子准备大干一番，当女婿推起割草机，他使出了吃奶的力气，可是割草机就是不听使唤，不愿意工作。原来是女婿没有拉开燃油箱，割草机怎么肯走呢？好不容易把草割完，女婿坐在一边，手拿一块干毛巾，在脸上、脖子上狠狠地擦拭着，喝了几口水，又举起了沉甸甸的长柄剪刀向枯黄的树枝发力。没剪几次，他的双手就支撑不住了，汗水从额前往下直淌，女婿用发软的手臂扶正眼镜，但手指不听命令，眼镜「哐啷」一声坠落在地，镜片碎了。

女儿气呼呼地责怪女婿不小心，然后费劲地把地上的杂草、烂叶以及几根枯枝装进垃圾桶，草草收兵，二人精疲力尽，像泄了气的皮球，瘫软在沙发上。

隔天两人全身筋骨酸痛，一周后才逐渐恢复。女婿说：「这割草的活我干不了，几十块钱不能省。」从此，「阿米哥」班每周一次来收拾后院，他为人老实又精力旺盛，干活敏捷，动作利索，不到半小时的时间，就把前庭后院打扫得干净整洁。四周的树木修剪得整齐有序，树枝上的花朶在树梢上争艳斗色地展显自我，草地一片绿油油，令人喜欢。

每次班收拾好工具离去时，我总会拿点水果或小点心慰劳他，叫他休息一下再走，他总是满脸笑容地摇摇手。久而之久，我们渐渐互相了解，不知不觉间默默地成了朋友。

一天早晨，来割草的不是班，而是他的儿子强，今年才十六岁。强告诉我们，他的老爸爬到一棵高大的树上整理树枝，一不小心，连人带树枝摔倒在地，二根肋骨骨折，而且刺伤了肺叶。强又说，以后这里的活由他来做，等他爸恢复了再来。我们知道后，心中为班担忧，女儿买了水果和恢复骨骼的营养品，让强带回去转交给他父亲。

往后的岁月里，都由强担任我们家的庭园工作，班只能干点轻微的小活。一天，班出现在后花园，他弯着腰默默修理着草地上的喷水龙头，我们上前和他招呼，并要付钱给他，但无论如何他不接受，只说：小事小事。无奈之下，我们只能悄悄地给强涨工资。

后来我们买了[**学区**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/学区)房，要搬家了，强心里非常难过。女婿打趣地说：「老房子出租，你照旧打理，我们会把你捆绑在一起带过去的。」在新房子的花园里，我们又见面了，我们和强脸上都泛出了笑容。

## English Translation
“Amigo” is a respectful and friendly term that Mexicans use to address a friend. Saying “friend” upon meeting someone for the first time brings a deep warmth to one's heart. I never expected that after living in the United States for over thirty years, I would become close friends with an “amigo” named Ben.

More than thirty years ago, my daughter and son-in-law had just established a foothold in the United States. They bought a small, detached house in San Diego with three bedrooms and two bathrooms. Although the house was small, the backyard was quite large, featuring tall banana, willow, and coconut trees, a wide lawn, and a small hill at the back.

In autumn, the leaves turned yellow and blew all over the backyard, and withered branches hung limply from the tree trunks. The weeds, wild grass, and green grass on the lawn cropped up unevenly under the sunlight, and the small hill was also overgrown with weeds, presenting a scene of utter chaos.

My daughter said that this sluggish, messy, and terrible environment had to be cleaned up quickly. At that time, their income was modest, and they were not wealthy, so to save money, they had to do this rough work themselves. One day, my daughter and son-in-law changed into old work clothes, rolled up their sleeves, and prepared to make a major effort. When my son-in-law pushed the lawnmower, he used every ounce of strength he had, but the mower simply would not cooperate and refused to work. It turned out that my son-in-law hadn't opened the fuel tank valve—how could the lawnmower possibly run? After finally finishing the mowing with great difficulty, my son-in-law sat to one side. Holding a dry towel, he wiped his face and neck vigorously. After taking a few sips of water, he lifted the heavy, long-handled shears to tackle the withered yellow branches. Before he had snipped more than a few times, his arms could no longer support the weight. Sweat poured straight down from his forehead. Adjusting his glasses with a limp arm, his fingers failed to obey his command, and with a “clank,” the glasses fell to the ground, shattering the lenses.

My daughter crossly blamed my son-in-law for being careless, and then painstakingly stuffed the weeds, rotten leaves, and a few dry branches from the ground into the trash can before hastily calling it a day. Exhausted, the two of them deflated like popped balloons and slumped onto the sofa.

The next day, their entire bodies were sore, and it took a week for them to gradually recover. My son-in-law said, “I can't handle this mowing job. This couple of dozen dollars is not something we can save.” From then on, “Amigo” Ben came once a week to tidy up the backyard. He was honest, energetic, agile, and quick. In less than half an hour, he would clean and tidy both the front and back yards. The surrounding trees were neatly pruned, flowers bloomed on the treetops as if competing for attention, and the lawn was lush and green, which was very pleasing to look at.

Every time Ben finished packing his tools and was about to leave, I would always offer him some fruit or snacks as a treat and tell him to rest a bit before going. He would always wave his hand with a broad smile. Over time, we gradually got to know each other and, without realizing it, quietly became friends.

One morning, it wasn't Ben who came to mow the lawn, but his son, Qiang, who was only sixteen years old. Qiang told us that his dad had climbed up a tall tree to trim the branches and accidentally fell to the ground, branches and all, fracturing two ribs and puncturing his lung. Qiang added that he would do the work here from now on, and his father would return once he recovered. Hearing this, we felt worried for Ben. My daughter bought some fruits and bone-healing supplements, asking Qiang to take them back and hand them to his father.

In the years that followed, Qiang took over our yard work, and Ben could only do light chores. One day, Ben appeared in the backyard, bending over to silently repair a lawn sprinkler. We went over to greet him and tried to pay him, but no matter what, he wouldn't accept it, only saying, “It's nothing, it's nothing.” Left with no choice, we quietly gave Qiang a raise.

Later, we bought a house in a good school district and had to move. Qiang felt very sad. My son-in-law joked, “We are renting out the old house, so you can keep taking care of it. We'll carry you along with us.” In the garden of our new house, we met again, and smiles appeared on both our faces and Qiang's.
