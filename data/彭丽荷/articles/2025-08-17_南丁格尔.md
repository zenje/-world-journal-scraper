# 南丁格尔

**Date:** 2025-08-17 02:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷
**URL:** https://www.worldjournal.com/wj/story/121252/8930619?zh-cn

晚饭过后，我和平时一样，在十时上床睡觉，半夜约一时左右，一阵阵腹痛袭来，我用双手按着腹部，又是揉揉又是搓搓，想用这种方法减轻一些疼痛，心想或许是胃肠道痉挛了，过一会儿就会缓解，可是这招不管用。约莫过了一个小时，阵阵疼痛变本加厉成为持续性的疼痛，我起身去拿止痛药，刚把药片放在嘴边，又想起医生曾经说过：病情没有确定以前，千万不要随便吃止痛药，因为它会让腹痛真正的原因被掩盖，医生不能作正确的判断。

我把药片放下，想呼唤住在楼上的女儿，但想到她上班忙了一天，很辛苦，不忍叫醒她。这时我感到恶心，一阵呕吐，想着吃坏肚子的东西都吐出来，这下应该可以了，不料疼痛不减，我只能忍着煎熬到天亮。

女儿醒来后赶忙送我去史克里普斯医院（Scripps Hospital），并埋怨我应该早早叫醒她。经过各种检查和化验，还做了CT，诊断为肠阻塞，即刻转入病房。医生提供两种治疗方案：使用药物让肠子慢慢打开，打不开就要动手术。

此时，我必须禁食、禁水。为了减轻胃肠道产生的胃液压力，一定要插胃管，接上引流器，把胃液时不时地抽出来。这时来了一名中年的白人护士，她站在我的床位旁边，用手轻轻地抚摸着我的肩膀，轻声细语地说：「别害怕，不要紧张，我会轻轻地把这根管子送进你的胃内，但你必须配合我，这样可以减少你的不适。」一根像原子笔粗细的橡皮管从我的鼻腔经过喉咙，插入食道再进入胃部，此时要大口吸气和作吞咽动作，虽然是个很痛苦、艰难的过程，但是在她的鼓励下，我很快顺利地完成了。

这名白人护士约莫三十岁出头，名叫Rose。她额头饱满，高高的鼻梁两侧有着一双会说话的蓝眼睛，特别有灵气；金黄色的头发扎成一个马尾辫，在忙碌中不停地摆动着。她做事干净俐落，井然有序，尤其讲话时总是带着微笑，轻声细语，耐心仔细地关心每个病人。

我身上插的管子较多，她特别用心照顾，因为我无法去厕所，Rose拿来了个座椅，下面有便盆，微笑地对我说：「你要解手的话，只要按铃，我马上就来帮助你。」每当我解手完毕，她更会轻声地说：「你今天的任务完成得很好。」

Rose每天帮我从鼻腔的管子里打药水时，总是耐心地说：「别紧张，我给你壮胆，你抓住我的胳膊就行，有不舒服的感觉可以举手」，在她的温柔照护下，不知不觉药物就注射完毕。通过Rose的精心护理，我治愈的信心加倍了，四天之后，我放了一个屁，Rose知道后，她欢呼跳跃起来，比我还高兴，嘴里称赞我：「你真棒，你的肠胃道修复好了。」

我开始可吃流质食物，她在旁边指导我慢慢吞咽、少量进食，再逐渐增加饭量。等到我想出院，Rose还是坚持要见到我有大便才能放行，这样才证明我的肠胃真正通畅了，才能放心让我回家。当我要和Rose分别时，她用轮椅送我到汽车旁，我俩依依不舍挥手告别。我对Rose说：「我要把南丁格尔这个优秀的称号送给您。」

## Nightingale
After dinner, as usual, I went to bed at ten o'clock. Around one o'clock in the middle of the night, waves of abdominal pain hit me. I pressed my abdomen with both hands, rubbing and massaging it, hoping to alleviate some of the pain this way. I thought it might just be a gastrointestinal spasm that would subside after a while, but this trick didn't work. After about an hour, the bouts of pain intensified into a continuous ache. I got up to get some pain relievers, but just as I put the tablet to my mouth, I remembered what a doctor had once said: never take pain medication before a condition is diagnosed, because it can mask the real cause of the abdominal pain, preventing doctors from making an accurate diagnosis.

I put the pill down and wanted to call my daughter who lived upstairs, but thinking of how hard she had worked all day, I couldn't bear to wake her. At that moment, I felt nauseated and vomited. I thought that throwing up whatever had upset my stomach would make it better, but to my dismay, the pain did not subside. I had no choice but to endure the agonizing pain until dawn.

After my daughter woke up, she rushed me to Scripps Hospital, complaining that I should have woken her earlier. Following various examinations, laboratory tests, and a CT scan, I was diagnosed with a bowel obstruction and immediately admitted to a ward. The doctor offered two treatment options: use medication to slowly open up the intestines, or, if that failed, perform surgery.

At this point, I had to fast completely, avoiding both food and water. To reduce the pressure of the gastric juices produced by the gastrointestinal tract, a nasogastric tube had to be inserted and connected to a drainage device to pump out stomach fluids from time to time. Just then, a middle-aged white nurse came in. Standing by my bedside, she gently stroked my shoulder and said in a soft, gentle voice, "Don't be afraid, don't be nervous. I will gently feed this tube into your stomach, but you must cooperate with me to minimize your discomfort." A rubber tube about the thickness of a ballpoint pen was inserted through my nasal cavity, past my throat, down my esophagus, and into my stomach. At that moment, I had to take deep breaths and swallow. Although it was a painful and difficult process, under her encouragement, I completed it quickly and smoothly.

This white nurse, in her early thirties, was named Rose. She had a full forehead, and on either side of her high nose bridge was a pair of expressive blue eyes that were exceptionally lively. Her golden-blonde hair was tied into a ponytail that swayed constantly amidst her busy routines. She worked efficiently and in an orderly fashion; moreover, she always spoke with a smile in a soft, gentle voice, caring for every patient with patience and attentiveness.

Since I had quite a few tubes attached to me, she took extra care of me. Because I couldn't go to the restroom, Rose brought over a bedside commode with a bedpan underneath. Smiling, she said to me, "If you need to relieve yourself, just ring the bell, and I'll come right away to help you." Every time I finished, she would say softly, "You completed today's task beautifully."

Every day, when Rose administered medication through my nasal tube, she would always say patiently, "Don't be nervous. I'm here to give you courage; just hold onto my arm, and raise your hand if you feel any discomfort." Under her gentle care, the injection was completed before I knew it. Thanks to Rose's meticulous care, my confidence in recovery doubled. Four days later, I passed gas. When Rose found out, she jumped for joy, even happier than I was, praising me, "You're wonderful! Your gastrointestinal tract has healed."

I was allowed to start on liquid food, and she guided me by my side to swallow slowly and eat in small amounts, gradually increasing my intake. When I wanted to be discharged, Rose still insisted on waiting until she saw that I had a bowel movement before letting me go, as this would prove my digestive tract was truly clear and she could comfortably let me go home. When it was time to part with Rose, she wheeled me to the car, and we waved goodbye, reluctant to part. I said to Rose, "I want to bestow upon you the honorable title of Nightingale."
