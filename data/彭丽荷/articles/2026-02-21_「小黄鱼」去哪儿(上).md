# 「小黄鱼」去哪儿(上)

**Date:** 2026-02-21 01:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷

上海人往往喜欢把小金条称为「小黄鱼」。黄金的色泽耀眼，像金色的阳光明媚灿烂，让人们心中充满了亮堂堂的色彩。上海人喜爱吃小黄鱼，牠不尽小巧玲珑、肉质细嫩鲜美，背部的颜色和黄金几乎相似，因此上海人特别宠爱，小金条就有了「小黄鱼」的爱称。上海人家庭一般都会收藏几条「小黄鱼」象征招财进宝，另外也能保值，遇到突发事件还能拿出来应个急，深受广大群众的青睐。

文化大革命初期，红卫兵抄家，以前过年喜气洋洋闹年锣的声音，如今变成了惊心动魂、恐惧刺耳的哀嚎。红卫兵拿起了原本人们喜爱的乐器，把这锣鼓充当祸害人们的武器，他们从这条街冲向那个巷，人们只要一听到这「闹年锣」的声音，家家户户都紧闭大门，惊恐万分，生怕恶运临头。

当锣鼓声极力地敲响之后，停在哪家门口，那家就倒霉了。被抄家的家人躲在一边，不敢吱声，眼睁睁地看着红卫兵耀武扬威、横七竖八地摆布、破坏，翻箱倒柜，任意折腾，只要是个像样的东西都被撕碎剪烂，说什么是封资修的产物，不破不立。

一天，锣鼓喧天的声音停在我同学家门口，红卫兵蜂拥直冲屋内，全面搜查，并且要求交出所有的帐本，查看他家赚了多少「黑心钱」。

这名同学家是开米行的，生意规模不算小，家境富裕，家中家具和摆式都很考究，唯独有一张老式的床，看上去比较陈旧，是她家老祖母长年睡的。老祖母八十多岁，横七竖八的皱纹布满了面庞，说话时嘴巴一瘪一瘪的样子，是个老态龙钟、名副其实的老妪。

当老祖母看到红卫兵冲到卧室时，人从床上吓得滚到了地板上，红卫兵不管她的死活，只顾翻开她的箱子，把东西统统倒出来，还注视着每个角落看哪些东西是四旧，要把它们砸个稀巴烂，彻底铲除干净。一个红卫兵在忙乱之中，不慎踩上了老祖母的手，痛得她躺在地板上「哇哇」大叫，同学父亲闻声把老母亲扶起躺回床上。

红卫兵在他家折腾了一阵，把箱子、家具之类的物品统统搬上车，说是箱子里都是奇装异服，家具是资产阶级享受的东西，全部拿走。红卫兵要来搬老祖母的床，同学父亲苦苦哀求，老祖母也用瘪瘪的嘴说：「请天兵天将行行好，留下这个破旧的床，让我有个安身之地。」那个踩老祖母手掌的红卫兵看了看她，说了一句「别啰嗦」，就跟另外几个红卫兵使了个眼神，这个破床总算留下了。红卫兵扬长而去，同学家一片哀嚎，老祖母摆摆手叫大家别太难过，她在愤恨中露出了一丝淡淡的苦笑。

老祖母的那个破旧老床，两边床架有四个柱子作为四个床腿，四个柱子顶端上都有一个小圆球，其中一根柱子上的小圆球是用镙丝帽固定的，可以旋转开来，这根柱子是空心的；另外三个小圆球则是跟柱子长在一起，转不开、拔不出来的。

老祖母抖抖颤颤使劲地捻开了那颗小圆球，叫家人们看看柱子洞里有什么，一阵惊呼，但很快都捂住了自己的嘴，原来老祖母存攒的「小黄鱼」在这儿避难。

某日，姨妈的大女儿到我家来说，她家虽然没有被抄，但她母亲已经被吓得、气得病倒了。原来姨妈省吃俭用攒了一点钱，买了几条「小黄鱼」，正愁着让它们游到什么地方去才安全。那时候每户人家烧饭都用煤球炉子，一个个黑黑的小圆煤球作为燃烧的原料，不妨把「小黄鱼」埋到煤球内暂时躲避。

姨妈想到后马上动手，把煤球捣开，把「小黄鱼」送进煤球肚子里面，再恢复原样，在外观略做了一点小记号，旁人是看不出来的，她还特意放在装煤球木箱的后面墙角旁。这个生煤炉、加煤球的活是姨妈一人操作的，所以她为选择这个方法挺得意的。她天天都守候着这几个煤球，没有人注意，也没有人知道她的这番操作。

一天姨妈的小女儿慌忙地跑回家来，说她的儿子遇到了车祸，昏迷不醒，在医院抢救，请求母亲去她那儿帮忙几天。姨妈一听自己的外孙遇到了这种情况，不加思索地答应，赶紧离开了自己的家。过了四、五天，外孙情况得到了好转，姨妈才想起了煤球，匆匆忙忙回了家。

不好！怎么带有小记号的煤球不见了，一下子她急得昏头转向，颤颤巍巍地把每个煤球都捣开来检查一遍，折腾一天，什么也没有。她瘫软坐在地上，用拳头直敲自已的胸口。晚上儿子、媳妇下班回家，她问媳妇是否每天点火生煤炉烧饭？媳妇应声答道：「是的。」她还很讨好姨妈地说着：「我把几个煤球从木箱后面扫出来，顺便把周围都打扫了一下，现在看上去干净了很多。」姨妈哑巴吃黄连，还不能吐露心里的气愤和难受。

一连几天，姨妈在煤炉撤灰时，总是仔仔细细地摄着一把把的灰，怀着能见到「小黄鱼」复活的希望，最后姨妈人消瘦了一圈，好几天都起不了床。后来听街坊邻居说，垃圾堆里可以捡到黄金，果真不错。姨妈家的「小黄鱼」你去哪儿啦？（上）

## English Translation
Where Did the "Little Yellow Croakers" Go? (Part 1)

Date: 2026-02-21 01:00 ET
Author: Peng Lihe

People in Shanghai often like to refer to small gold bars as "little yellow croakers" (小黄鱼). The dazzling luster of gold is like bright, brilliant sunshine, filling people's hearts with cheerful colors. Shanghainese love to eat little yellow croaker fish; not only is it compact, delicate, and delicious, but the color of its back is almost identical to that of gold. Thus, it is highly favored by locals, and small gold bars earned the affectionate nickname "little yellow croakers." Shanghai families generally keep a few "little yellow croakers" at home to symbolize attracting wealth and treasure, as well as to preserve value. They can also be taken out to help in emergencies, making them highly popular among the general public.

In the early stages of the Cultural Revolution, the Red Guards began raiding homes. The once joyful and festive sounds of New Year's gongs and drums were now replaced by soul-stirring, terrifying, and ear-piercing wails. The Red Guards took up the musical instruments that people once loved, using these gongs and drums as weapons to torment them. They rushed from street to street, and from alley to alley. Whenever people heard the sound of these "New Year gongs," every household would tightly lock their doors in sheer terror, fearing that misfortune would fall upon them.

When the drums and gongs were beaten violently, whichever house they stopped in front of was in for bad luck. The family whose home was being raided would hide to one side, not daring to make a sound, watching helplessly as the Red Guards threw their weight around, arbitrarily rearranging and destroying things, rummaging through chests and cupboards, and wreaking havoc. Anything decent was ripped to shreds or cut apart, labeled as "products of feudalism, capitalism, and revisionism" that had to be destroyed because "without destruction, there can be no construction."

One day, the deafening sound of drums and gongs stopped in front of my classmate's house. Red Guards surged into the home, conducting an all-out search, and demanded they hand over all account books to see how much "dirty money" the family had made.

This classmate's family ran a rice mill. Their business was quite large, and they were wealthy. The furniture and decorations in their home were very exquisite, except for an old-fashioned bed that looked rather worn, which their elderly grandmother had slept in for many years. The grandmother was in her eighties, her face covered with crisscrossing wrinkles, and her mouth puckered when she spoke. She was a truly decrepit, elderly woman.

When the grandmother saw the Red Guards rush into her bedroom, she fell from her bed to the floor in terror. Ignoring whether she lived or died, the Red Guards focused on flipping over her trunks, dumping everything out, and eyeing every corner to see what counted as the "Four Olds" so they could smash them to pieces and wipe them out completely. In the chaos, a Red Guard accidentally stepped on the grandmother's hand. Screaming in pain on the floor, she was helped back onto the bed by the classmate's father, who had heard her cries.

After ransacking the house for a while, the Red Guards loaded the trunks, furniture, and other items onto a truck, claiming the trunks were filled with "exotic and bizarre dress" and the furniture represented "bourgeois enjoyment," and took them all away. When they moved to take the grandmother's bed, the classmate's father begged desperately, and the grandmother also said with her puckered mouth, "Please, heavenly soldiers, have mercy and leave this broken old bed so I have a place to rest my head." The Red Guard who had stepped on her hand looked at her, muttered "Stop nagging," gave a look to the other Red Guards, and ultimately left the broken bed behind. The Red Guards swaggered away, leaving the classmate's family in wails of grief. The grandmother waved her hand, telling everyone not to be too sad, a faint, bitter smile appearing on her face amid her resentment.

That old, broken bed of the grandmother's had four pillars at the corners serving as bedposts. At the top of each pillar was a small round wooden ball. The ball on one of the pillars was fixed with a screw cap and could be unscrewed; this pillar was hollow inside. The other three balls were integrated with the pillars and could not be turned or pulled out.

Trembling, the grandmother twisted open the small ball with great effort and asked her family to look inside the hollow pillar. A gasp of surprise arose, but they quickly covered their mouths—as it turned out, this was where the grandmother's saved "little yellow croakers" had taken refuge.

One day, my aunt's eldest daughter came to our house and said that although her home had not been raided, her mother had fallen ill from fear and anger. It turned out that my aunt had saved some money through thrift and bought a few "little yellow croakers," and she was worrying about where they could "swim" to be safe. At that time, every household used coal briquettes for cooking. These small, black, round briquettes served as fuel, and she thought she might temporarily hide the "little yellow croakers" inside the briquettes.

The aunt acted immediately. She broke open some coal briquettes, stuffed the "little yellow croakers" into their bellies, restored them to their original shape, made a tiny, unnoticeable mark on the outside, and carefully placed them in the corner behind the wooden box holding the briquettes. Since making the fire and adding coal was a chore only my aunt did, she felt quite proud of this method. She watched over these briquettes every day; no one noticed, and no one knew of her secret operation.

One day, the aunt's younger daughter rushed home, saying her son had been in a car accident, was unconscious, and was being treated in the hospital. She begged her mother to come help her for a few days. Hearing that her grandson was in such a state, the aunt agreed without a second thought and rushed out of the house. After four or five days, when the grandson's condition improved, the aunt finally remembered the coal briquettes and hurried back home.

Oh no! The marked coal briquettes were gone! Suddenly, she became dizzy with anxiety. Trembling, she broke open every single coal briquette to check, tossing and turning them all day, but found nothing. She collapsed onto the floor, beating her chest with her fists. That evening, when her son and daughter-in-law came home from work, she asked her daughter-in-law if she had lit the coal stove to cook every day. The daughter-in-law replied helpfully, "Yes, I did! I swept out a few coal briquettes from behind the wooden box and cleaned up the area while I was at it. It looks much cleaner now!" The aunt had to suffer in silence, unable to voice her anger and grief.

For several consecutive days, when clearing the ashes from the coal stove, the aunt carefully sifted through hands full of ash, holding onto the hope of seeing the "little yellow croakers" resurrect. In the end, she lost a significant amount of weight and couldn't get out of bed for several days. Later, she heard neighbors say that gold could indeed be found in the trash heaps. Oh, "little yellow croakers" of my aunt's family, where did you go? (Part 1)
