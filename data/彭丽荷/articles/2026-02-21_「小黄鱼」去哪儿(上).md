# 「小黄鱼」去哪儿(上)

**Date:** 2026-02-21 01:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷

上海人往往喜欢把小金条称为「小黄鱼」。黄金的色泽耀眼，像金色的阳光明媚灿烂，让人们心中充满了亮堂堂的色彩。上海人喜爱吃小黄鱼，牠不尽小巧玲珑、肉质细嫩鲜美，背部的颜色和黄金几乎相似，因此上海人特别宠爱，小金条就有了「小黄鱼」的爱称。上海人家庭一般都会收藏几条「小黄鱼」象征招财进宝，另外也能保值，遇到突发事件还能拿出来应个急，深受广大群众的青睐。

文化大革命初期，红卫兵抄家，以前过年喜气洋洋闹年锣的声音，如今变成了惊心动魂、恐惧刺耳的哀嚎。红卫兵拿起了原本人们喜爱的乐器，把这锣鼓充当祸害人们的武器，他们从这条街冲向那个巷，人们只要一听到这「闹年锣」的声音，家家户户都紧闭大门，惊恐万分，生怕恶运临头。

当锣鼓声极力地敲响之后，停在哪家门口，那家就倒霉了。被抄家的家人躲在一边，不敢吱声，眼睁睁地看着红卫兵耀武扬威、横七竖八地摆布、破坏，翻箱倒柜，任意折腾，只要是个像样的东西都被撕碎剪烂，说什么是封资修的产物，不破不立。

一天，锣鼓喧天的声音停在我同学家门口，红卫兵蜂拥直冲屋内，全面搜查，并且要求交出所有的帐本，查看他家赚了多少「黑心钱」。

这名同学家是开米行的，生意规模不算小，家境富裕，家中家具和摆式都很考究，唯独有一张老式的床，看上去比较陈旧，是她家老祖母长年睡的。老祖母八十多岁，横七竖八的皱纹布满了面庞，说话时嘴巴一瘪一瘪的样子，是个老态龙钟、名副其实的老妪。

当老祖母看到红卫兵冲到卧室时，人从床上吓得滚到了地板上，红卫兵不管她的死活，只顾翻开她的箱子，把东西统统倒出来，还注视着每个角落看哪些东西是四旧，要把它们砸个稀巴烂，彻底铲除干净。一个红卫兵在忙乱之中，不慎踩上了老祖母的手，痛得她躺在地板上「哇哇」大叫，同学父亲闻声把老母亲扶起躺回床上。

红卫兵在他家折腾了一阵，把箱子、家具之类的物品统统搬上车，说是箱子里都是奇装异服，家具是资产阶级享受的东西，全部拿走。红卫兵要来搬老祖母的床，同学父亲苦苦哀求，老祖母也用瘪瘪的嘴说：「请天兵天将行行好，留下这个破旧的床，让我有个安身之地。」那个踩老祖母手掌的红卫兵看了看她，说了一句「别啰嗦」，就跟另外几个红卫兵使了个眼神，这个破床总算留下了。红卫兵扬长而去，同学家一片哀嚎，老祖母摆摆手叫大家别太难过，她在愤恨中露出了一丝淡淡的苦笑。

老祖母的那个破旧老床，两边床架有四个柱子作为四个床腿，四个柱子顶端上都有一个小圆球，其中一根柱子上的小圆球是用镙丝帽固定的，可以旋转开来，这根柱子是空心的；另外三个小圆球则是跟柱子长在一起，转不开、拔不出来的。

老祖母抖抖颤颤使劲地捻开了那颗小圆球，叫家人们看看柱子洞里有什么，一阵惊呼，但很快都捂住了自己的嘴，原来老祖母存攒的「小黄鱼」在这儿避难。

某日，姨妈的大女儿到我家来说，她家虽然没有被抄，但她母亲已经被吓得、气得病倒了。原来姨妈省吃俭用攒了一点钱，买了几条「小黄鱼」，正愁着让它们游到什么地方去才安全。那时候每户人家烧饭都用煤球炉子，一个个黑黑的小圆煤球作为燃烧的原料，不妨把「小黄鱼」埋到煤球内暂时躲避。

姨妈想到后马上动手，把煤球捣开，把「小黄鱼」送进煤球肚子里面，再恢复原样，在外观略做了一点小记号，旁人是看不出来的，她还特意放在装煤球木箱的后面墙角旁。这个生煤炉、加煤球的活是姨妈一人操作的，所以她为选择这个方法挺得意的。她天天都守候着这几个煤球，没有人注意，也没有人知道她的这番操作。

一天姨妈的小女儿慌忙地跑回家来，说她的儿子遇到了车祸，昏迷不醒，在医院抢救，请求母亲去她那儿帮忙几天。姨妈一听自己的外孙遇到了这种情况，不加思索地答应，赶紧离开了自己的家。过了四、五天，外孙情况得到了好转，姨妈才想起了煤球，匆匆忙忙回了家。

不好！怎么带有小记号的煤球不见了，一下子她急得昏头转向，颤颤巍巍地把每个煤球都捣开来检查一遍，折腾一天，什么也没有。她瘫软坐在地上，用拳头直敲自已的胸口。晚上儿子、媳妇下班回家，她问媳妇是否每天点火生煤炉烧饭？媳妇应声答道：「是的。」她还很讨好姨妈地说着：「我把几个煤球从木箱后面扫出来，顺便把周围都打扫了一下，现在看上去干净了很多。」姨妈哑巴吃黄连，还不能吐露心里的气愤和难受。

一连几天，姨妈在煤炉撤灰时，总是仔仔细细地摄着一把把的灰，怀着能见到「小黄鱼」复活的希望，最后姨妈人消瘦了一圈，好几天都起不了床。后来听街坊邻居说，垃圾堆里可以捡到黄金，果真不错。姨妈家的「小黄鱼」你去哪儿啦？（上）

## English Translation
Where Did the "Little Yellow Croakers" Go? (Part 1)

Shanghainese people like to refer to small gold bars as "little yellow croakers." The dazzling luster of gold is like bright, warm sunshine, filling people's hearts with a brilliant glow. Shanghainese people love to eat little yellow croakers; not only are they delicate and delicious, but their back color is almost identical to gold. Thus, Shanghainese people have a special affection for them, giving small gold bars the endearing nickname "little yellow croakers." Shanghai families typically keep a few "little yellow croakers" to symbolize ushering in wealth and prosperity, as well as preserving value and serving as emergency funds in times of unexpected crisis. They are highly favored by the general public.

In the early days of the Cultural Revolution, the Red Guards raided homes. The once-joyful, festive sounds of the "New Year gongs" turned into heart-stopping, terrifying, and piercing wails. The Red Guards took up these musical instruments that people once loved and turned the gongs and drums into weapons of persecution. They rushed from one street to another. As soon as people heard the sound of these "New Year gongs," every household bolted their doors in extreme panic, fearing that misfortune would fall upon them.

When the drums and gongs clanged intensely and stopped outside a house, that family was doomed. The residents of the raided home would hide to one side, not daring to make a sound, watching helplessly as the arrogant Red Guards threw their weight around, turning things upside down, breaking items, ransacking chests and cupboards, and making an absolute mess. Anything decent was torn to shreds or cut to pieces under the pretext that it was a product of "feudalism, capitalism, and revisionism" and that "there is no construction without destruction."

One day, the deafening sound of drums and gongs stopped in front of my classmate's house. Red Guards swarmed into the home, conducting a thorough search and demanding that all account books be handed over to see how much "dirty money" the family had made.

My classmate's family ran a rice shop. It was a reasonably large business, and they were quite wealthy. Their furniture and home decor were very exquisite, except for an old-fashioned bed that looked rather worn, which her elderly grandmother had slept in for years. The grandmother was over eighty years old, her face covered in deep wrinkles, and her mouth puckered when she spoke—a truly decrepit, elderly woman.

When the grandmother saw the Red Guards barge into her bedroom, she fell out of bed onto the floor in terror. Ignoring whether she lived or died, the Red Guards focused on turning her chests inside out, dumping everything, and searching every corner to see what qualified as "Four Olds" so they could smash them to smithereens and eliminate them completely. In the chaos, one Red Guard accidentally stepped on the grandmother's hand, causing her to cry out in pain on the floor. Hearing her cries, the classmate's father helped his elderly mother back onto the bed.

After tossing the house upside down for a while, the Red Guards loaded chests, furniture, and other items onto a truck, claiming the chests contained "bizarre dress" and the furniture represented "bourgeois indulgence," so everything had to be confiscated. When they went to take the grandmother's bed, the classmate's father begged piteously, and the grandmother also used her puckered mouth to say, "Please, heavenly soldiers, be merciful and leave this broken old bed so I have a place to rest." The Red Guard who had stepped on her hand looked at her, muttered "Stop whining," winked at the other Red Guards, and ultimately spared the broken bed. As the Red Guards swaggered off, the classmate's home was filled with wails. The grandmother waved her hand, telling everyone not to be too sad, and let out a faint, bitter smile amidst her resentment.

The grandmother's old, dilapidated bed had four posts at the corners acting as legs. At the top of each post was a small wooden sphere. The sphere on one of the posts was secured with a screw cap and could be twisted off; this post was hollow. The other three spheres were carved directly onto the posts and could not be twisted or pulled off.

Trembling, the grandmother twisted open that small sphere with great effort and asked her family to look inside the hollow post. There was a collective gasp, but they quickly covered their mouths. It turned out that the "little yellow croakers" the grandmother had saved up over the years were taking refuge there.

One day, my aunt’s eldest daughter came to our house and said that although her home had not been raided, her mother had fallen ill from fright and anger. It turned out that my aunt had saved up some money through frugal living to buy a few "little yellow croakers," and she was worrying about where they could "swim" to be safe. At that time, every household used coal briquettes for cooking. These small, black, round briquettes served as fuel. She thought she might as well bury the "little yellow croakers" inside the coal briquettes to temporarily hide them.

Once the idea struck her, the aunt immediately set to work. She broke open the briquettes, stuffed the "little yellow croakers" inside, restored them to their original shape, made a tiny, subtle mark on the outside that others wouldn't notice, and deliberately placed them in a corner behind the wooden box holding the coal briquettes. Since lighting the stove and adding coal briquettes was a job only my aunt did, she was quite proud of this method. She watched over those briquettes every day; no one paid any attention, and no one knew of her secret operation.

One day, my aunt’s younger daughter rushed home, saying her son had been in a car accident, was unconscious, and was being treated in the intensive care unit. She begged her mother to come and help for a few days. Upon hearing of her grandson's situation, the aunt agreed without a second thought and hurriedly left her home. After four or five days, her grandson's condition improved, and only then did the aunt remember the coal briquettes and rush back home.

Oh no! How could the briquettes with the little marks be gone? Suddenly, she was dizzy with anxiety. Trembling, she broke open every single coal briquette to check, spending the entire day searching, but found nothing. She collapsed onto the floor, beating her chest with her fists. That evening, when her son and daughter-in-law returned from work, she asked her daughter-in-law if she had lit the stove and cooked with the coal briquettes every day. The daughter-in-law replied, "Yes," and added, trying to please her, "I swept up those few coal briquettes from behind the wooden box and cleaned up the surrounding area while I was at it. It looks much cleaner now!" The aunt had to swallow her bitterness in silence, unable to voice her anger and grief.

For several days in a row, when cleaning out the ashes from the stove, my aunt would carefully sift through every handful of ash, harboring the hope that she might see her "little yellow croakers" rise from the ashes. In the end, she lost a significant amount of weight and was bedridden for several days. Later, she heard neighbors say that gold could indeed be found in trash heaps. Oh, "little yellow croakers" of my aunt's house, where did you go? (Part 1)
