# 「小黄鱼」去哪儿(上)

**Date:** 2026-02-21 01:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷
**URL:** https://www.worldjournal.com/wj/story/121251/9318811?zh-cn

上海人往往喜欢把小金条称为「小黄鱼」。黄金的色泽耀眼，像金色的阳光明媚灿烂，让人们心中充满了亮堂堂的色彩。上海人喜爱吃小黄鱼，牠不尽小巧玲珑、肉质细嫩鲜美，背部的颜色和黄金几乎相似，因此上海人特别宠爱，小金条就有了「小黄鱼」的爱称。上海人家庭一般都会收藏几条「小黄鱼」象征招财进宝，另外也能保值，遇到突发事件还能拿出来应个急，深受广大群众的青睐。

文化大革命初期，红卫兵抄家，以前过年喜气洋洋闹年锣的声音，如今变成了惊心动魂、恐惧刺耳的哀嚎。红卫兵拿起了原本人们喜爱的乐器，把这锣鼓充当祸害人们的武器，他们从这条街冲向那个巷，人们只要一听到这「闹年锣」的声音，家家户户都紧闭大门，惊恐万分，生怕恶运临头。

当锣鼓声极力地敲响之后，停在哪家门口，那家就倒霉了。被抄家的家人躲在一边，不敢吱声，眼睁睁地看着红卫兵耀武扬威、横七竖八地摆布、破坏，翻箱倒柜，任意折腾，只要是个像样的东西都被撕碎剪烂，说什么是封资修的产物，不破不立。

一天，锣鼓喧天的声音停在我同学家门口，红卫兵蜂拥直冲屋内，全面搜查，并且要求交出所有的帐本，查看他家赚了多少「黑心钱」。

这名同学家是开米行的，生意规模不算小，家境富裕，家中家具和摆式都很考究，唯独有一张老式的床，看上去比较陈旧，是她家老祖母长年睡的。老祖母八十多岁，横七竖八的皱纹布满了面庞，说话时嘴巴一瘪一瘪的样子，是个老态龙钟、名副其实的老妪。

当老祖母看到红卫兵冲到卧室时，人从床上吓得滚到了地板上，红卫兵不管她的死活，只顾翻开她的箱子，把东西统统倒出来，还注视着每个角落看哪些东西是四旧，要把它们砸个稀巴烂，彻底铲除干净。一个红卫兵在忙乱之中，不慎踩上了老祖母的手，痛得她躺在地板上「哇哇」大叫，同学父亲闻声把老母亲扶起躺回床上。

红卫兵在他家折腾了一阵，把箱子、家具之类的物品统统搬上车，说是箱子里都是奇装异服，家具是资产阶级享受的东西，全部拿走。红卫兵要来搬老祖母的床，同学父亲苦苦哀求，老祖母也用瘪瘪的嘴说：「请天兵天将行行好，留下这个破旧的床，让我有个安身之地。」那个踩老祖母手掌的红卫兵看了看她，说了一句「别啰嗦」，就跟另外几个红卫兵使了个眼神，这个破床总算留下了。红卫兵扬长而去，同学家一片哀嚎，老祖母摆摆手叫大家别太难过，她在愤恨中露出了一丝淡淡的苦笑。

老祖母的那个破旧老床，两边床架有四个柱子作为四个床腿，四个柱子顶端上都有一个小圆球，其中一根柱子上的小圆球是用镙丝帽固定的，可以旋转开来，这根柱子是空心的；另外三个小圆球则是跟柱子长在一起，转不开、拔不出来的。

老祖母抖抖颤颤使劲地捻开了那颗小圆球，叫家人们看看柱子洞里有什么，一阵惊呼，但很快都捂住了自己的嘴，原来老祖母存攒的「小黄鱼」在这儿避难。

某日，姨妈的大女儿到我家来说，她家虽然没有被抄，但她母亲已经被吓得、气得病倒了。原来姨妈省吃俭用攒了一点钱，买了几条「小黄鱼」，正愁着让它们游到什么地方去才安全。那时候每户人家烧饭都用煤球炉子，一个个黑黑的小圆煤球作为燃烧的原料，不妨把「小黄鱼」埋到煤球内暂时躲避。

姨妈想到后马上动手，把煤球捣开，把「小黄鱼」送进煤球肚子里面，再恢复原样，在外观略做了一点小记号，旁人是看不出来的，她还特意放在装煤球木箱的后面墙角旁。这个生煤炉、加煤球的活是姨妈一人操作的，所以她为选择这个方法挺得意的。她天天都守候着这几个煤球，没有人注意，也没有人知道她的这番操作。

一天姨妈的小女儿慌忙地跑回家来，说她的儿子遇到了车祸，昏迷不醒，在医院抢救，请求母亲去她那儿帮忙几天。姨妈一听自己的外孙遇到了这种情况，不加思索地答应，赶紧离开了自己的家。过了四、五天，外孙情况得到了好转，姨妈才想起了煤球，匆匆忙忙回了家。

不好！怎么带有小记号的煤球不见了，一下子她急得昏头转向，颤颤巍巍地把每个煤球都捣开来检查一遍，折腾一天，什么也没有。她瘫软坐在地上，用拳头直敲自已的胸口。晚上儿子、媳妇下班回家，她问媳妇是否每天点火生煤炉烧饭？媳妇应声答道：「是的。」她还很讨好姨妈地说着：「我把几个煤球从木箱后面扫出来，顺便把周围都打扫了一下，现在看上去干净了很多。」姨妈哑巴吃黄连，还不能吐露心里的气愤和难受。

一连几天，姨妈在煤炉撤灰时，总是仔仔细细地摄着一把把的灰，怀着能见到「小黄鱼」复活的希望，最后姨妈人消瘦了一圈，好几天都起不了床。后来听街坊邻居说，垃圾堆里可以捡到黄金，果真不错。姨妈家的「小黄鱼」你去哪儿啦？（上）

## Where Did the 'Little Yellow Croakers' Go? (Part 1)
Shanghai people often like to call small gold bars 'little yellow croakers' (xiao huang yu). The dazzling luster of gold is like bright, brilliant sunshine, filling people's hearts with a luminous warmth. Shanghai people love to eat little yellow croakers; not only are they delicate, exquisite, and deliciously tender, but the color of their backs is almost identical to gold. Thus, Shanghai people hold them in special affection, and small gold bars came to have the affectionate nickname 'little yellow croakers.' Shanghai families would usually save a few 'little yellow croakers' as a symbol of attracting wealth and prosperity, as well as to preserve value, and in the event of a sudden emergency, they could be brought out to save the day, making them highly favored by the general public.

During the early days of the Cultural Revolution, the Red Guards searched and confiscated homes. The once joyful and celebratory sound of New Year's gongs and drums was now transformed into thrilling, terrifying, and ear-piercing wails. Red Guards seized the instruments that people once loved, using these gongs and drums as weapons to torment them. They rushed from street to alley, and as soon as people heard the sound of these 'New Year's gongs,' every household bolted their doors, gripped by terror, fearing that misfortune was about to strike.

When the gongs and drums banged violently, whichever house they stopped in front of was in for bad luck. The families whose homes were being raided cowered to one side, not daring to utter a sound, watching helplessly as the Red Guards, swaggering and chaotic, ordered them around, destroyed things, rummaged through chests and cupboards, and ran amok. Anything of decent quality was ripped to shreds and cut to pieces under the pretext of being products of 'feudalism, capitalism, and revisionism' that must be destroyed to make way for the new.

One day, the deafening sound of gongs and drums stopped at my classmate's house. Red Guards swarmed into the house, conducting a thorough search and demanding that all account books be handed over to see how much 'dirty money' her family had made.

This classmate's family ran a rice mill. The scale of their business was not small, and they were wealthy. The furniture and decorations in their home were very elegant, except for an old-fashioned bed that looked rather worn, which her grandmother had slept on for many years. The grandmother was in her eighties, her face covered in crisscrossing wrinkles, and her mouth puckered when she spoke—a truly decrepit, elderly woman.

When the grandmother saw the Red Guards barging into the bedroom, she fell out of bed onto the floor in terror. Ignoring whether she lived or died, the Red Guards focused on tossing open her chests, dumping everything out, and scrutinizing every corner to see what counted as the 'Four Olds' so they could smash them to pieces and eradicate them completely. In the chaos, a Red Guard accidentally stepped on the grandmother's hand, causing her to cry out in pain as she lay on the floor. Hearing her cries, the classmate's father helped his elderly mother back onto the bed.

After wrecking the house for a while, the Red Guards loaded the chests, furniture, and other items onto a truck, claiming that the chests contained exotic clothing and the furniture represented bourgeois indulgence, and took them all away. When the Red Guards went to move the grandmother's bed, the classmate's father begged desperately, and the grandmother also said with her puckered mouth, 'Please, heavenly soldiers and generals, show some mercy and leave this old, broken bed so I have a place to rest my head.' The Red Guard who had stepped on her hand looked at her, muttered 'Stop nagging,' and gave a look to the other Red Guards. The broken bed was ultimately left behind. The Red Guards swaggered away, leaving the classmate's family weeping in grief, while the grandmother waved her hand, telling everyone not to be too sad. Amidst her resentment, a faint, bitter smile appeared on her face.

The grandmother's old, broken bed had four posts at the sides serving as bed legs, each topped with a small round knob. On one post, the small knob was secured with a screw cap and could be twisted off; this post was hollow. The other three knobs were fused to the posts and could not be turned or pulled out.

Trembling, the grandmother twisted open that small knob with effort and told her family to look inside the hollow post. There was a gasp, but everyone quickly covered their mouths. It turned out that the 'little yellow croakers' the grandmother had saved were taking refuge there.

One day, my aunt's eldest daughter came to our house and said that although her home had not been searched, her mother had fallen ill from fear and anger. It turned out that her aunt had saved some money through frugality to buy a few 'little yellow croakers' and was worrying about where they could swim to safety. At that time, every household used coal briquettes as fuel for cooking. These small, black, round briquettes were the raw material for burning, so she thought she might as well bury the 'little yellow croakers' inside the briquettes to temporarily hide them.

Once the idea came to her, she immediately set to work. She broke open the briquettes, stuffed the 'little yellow croakers' inside, restored them to their original shape, and made a subtle mark on the outside that others could not notice. She even specially placed them in the corner behind the wooden box holding the briquettes. Lighting the stove and adding briquettes was a job she did alone, so she was quite pleased with her method. She watched over these few briquettes every day; no one paid any attention, and no one knew of her operation.

One day, her aunt's younger daughter ran home in a panic, saying that her son had been in a car accident, was unconscious, and was being treated in the hospital. She begged her mother to go help her for a few days. Hearing that her grandson was in such a state, the aunt agreed without a second thought and quickly left home. After four or five days, when her grandson's condition improved, the aunt remembered the briquettes and rushed back home.

Oh no! How could the briquettes with the small marks be gone? Suddenly, she was frantic with worry. Trembling, she broke open every single briquette to check, struggling all day, but found nothing. She collapsed onto the floor, beating her chest with her fists. That evening, when her son and daughter-in-law came home from work, she asked her daughter-in-law if she had lit the stove to cook every day. Her daughter-in-law replied, 'Yes.' Seeking her aunt's approval, she added, 'I swept those few briquettes out from behind the wooden box and cleaned up the surrounding area while I was at it. It looks much cleaner now.' Her aunt had to swallow her bitterness, unable to voice her anger and distress.

For several days in a row, when clearing the ashes from the stove, her aunt always carefully sifted through handfuls of ash, holding onto the hope of seeing the 'little yellow croakers' resurrected. In the end, she lost a significant amount of weight and could not get out of bed for several days. Later, she heard neighbors say that gold could be scavenged from the garbage dump—and indeed, it was true. Where did the 'little yellow croakers' of my aunt's family go? (Part 1)
