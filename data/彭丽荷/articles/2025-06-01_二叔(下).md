# 二叔(下)

**Date:** 2025-06-01 02:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷
**URL:** https://www.worldjournal.com/wj/story/121251/8763231?zh-cn

  

图/123RF

一九四九年，父亲把二叔安排到江西景德镇去帮忙办货，打理瓷器生意。二叔本性不是做生意的料，他好动、坐不住，喜欢体育运动，参加各种球模拟赛，各种球艺都很精通。他在篮球比赛中结交了一位外国神父，两人非常投缘，成为好朋友。在交往中，神父教二叔英文，他们用英文交流会话，二叔的英文水平突飞猛进，一口流利的英语让人羡慕，在神父的传教下，还成为了一名虔诚的信徒。

景德镇陶瓷商会主席是一名六十岁开外姓周的老先生，他圆圆的脸庞上总挂着笑容，三七开缝的头发，梳理得一丝不苟。每逢外出办事，周老先生身穿一件深褐色丝绸长衫，上面织有圆形寿字图案，外面套上优质面料的马褂，胸前左上方的小口袋里装上金色怀表，表上牵系着一条金链子，另一端紧紧扣住胸前的钮扣上。他戴了一副无框架的金水边眼镜，脚下蹬上一双棕黑色珵亮的皮鞋，手上拿了一根文明棍（拐杖），走起路来很气派，挺有绅士之风度。

周公馆豪华、气派，家庭富裕；周老先生有四儿两女，亲家都是豪门贵族。当周先生知道二叔是军人出身，又跟外国人有交往，并是单身，便想把他的二姑娘许配给二叔。

这位二姑娘芳龄二十三，五年之前，已订婚许配给一户赵姓财主家的儿子。姑娘到了二十岁那年准备出嫁，却突然遭遇不测风云，横祸降临，赵家的儿子在运送货物途中暴病身亡。二姑娘在家待了三年之久，没有人敢来提亲，说是这姑娘霉气很重，都害怕与她和合。眼看女儿的终身大事难以求成，这也急坏了周老先生。

在开陶瓷商会的时候，周先生特意和二叔亲切交互，并邀请二叔去他家作客，经过全方面的了解，周先生感到很满意，下定决心要套牢这位女婿。从此，好酒、好菜、好招待，二叔便成了他家的座上客。

周家不断在生活上照料二叔，还拿出不少资金援助，让他自立门户做瓷器生意，为他买楼置房。二叔不信邪、不怕鬼，什么晦气、霉气他全不在乎，这样的好运怎能抵挡得了？他也不征求母亲和大哥的意见，就要和周家的二姑娘结婚了。

周先生觉得二姑娘太受委屈，怕她婚后得不到尊重，因此把婚礼搞得特别气派、豪华。记得他的婚房是在一条大巷子里的别墅，布置风格奇特，中西结合，有宽敞的大客厅，客厅上方有一个宽长的条亭，条亭的前面有个红木八仙桌，两旁两把雕花椅子，左右两边也有椅子和茶几搭配；两面墙上悬挂着中国山水画和古色古香的条幅。

穿过大客厅，后面是自家的小客厅，放的都是皮沙发，沙发前面有小方台，配着精致的摆饰。窗台旁有个古老的留声机，不断传出悠悠动听的音乐；墙上挂的是西洋风景画和抽象派的油画，把整个气氛点缀得温馨、优雅。

迎亲那天，大巷子正前方出现了一座高大的彩牌楼，上面大红大紫艳丽的鲜花和各种各色的彩带、气球随风起舞，迎接着新娘到来。

新娘子坐在披着大红丝幛四人擡的花轿内，前面有洋鼓洋号的乐队奏曲，吹吹打打整齐的步伐慢慢前进着。后面紧跟着「十里红妆」，由两人一组，一前一后擡着沉甸甸的嫁妆缓步前行。随同新娘子的珠宝首饰盒里，装有珍珠项链、玉石配带和祖母绿戒指……。炮竹震天响，鞭炮满街舞，婚宴足足举办了三天三夜，热闹非凡。

解放后，二叔在政治上、生意上受到很大冲击，由于他有一股刚毅之气，也有心理准备，他总乐观地说：「我是个老运动员，每次运动来了，都少不了我。」又补充道：「一场大雨过后，有人看到的是一片彩虹，有人看到一地污泥。但是，我心中永远装满了希望。」

瓷器生意停摆，二叔一家来到上海，二叔被应聘到一所中学，成为一名英语教师。二叔一直认为上帝眷顾他，神父教他学会了英文，到了最困难的时候，英语竟派上了大用场。

改革开放了，在二叔[**退休**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/退休)之前，一天忽然来了一位老先生敲隔壁邻居的大门，大门久久未开，二叔见状，请老先生进他屋里稍坐，问清楚老先生的来意。原来，老先生是黄埔军校高年级的学生，他特地从美国回上海，寻找他很要好的老同学，看看能帮助他做点什么。

遗憾的是，邻居老同学在好多年前就过世了。老先生红着眼圈说：「真没想到，从此就见不到面了。」

当老先生知道二叔也是黄埔军校的同学，而且是某届某班的学生，他一时想起了低年级是有一个叫彭长鸣的人。他俩回忆当年学校的场景，有说不完的话，讲不尽的故事……。老先生真挚地问：「你有什么需要帮忙的？」不久，二叔携带着婶娘乘上了[**东方航空**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/东方航空)飞机，来到了美国，老先生为他们安排了衣食住行，过上后顾无忧的生活。

## Second Uncle (Part II)
In 1949, my father arranged for my second uncle to go to Jingdezhen, Jiangxi, to help source goods and manage the porcelain business. By nature, Second Uncle was not cut out for business; he was active, couldn't sit still, loved sports, and participated in various ball game simulations, mastering all kinds of ball skills. During a basketball game, he befriended a foreign priest. They hit it off immediately and became close friends. In their interactions, the priest taught Second Uncle English, and they conversed in English. Second Uncle's English level improved by leaps and bounds, making others envious of his fluent spoken English. Under the priest's missionary influence, he also became a devout believer.

The chairman of the Jingdezhen Ceramic Chamber of Commerce was an elderly gentleman in his sixties surnamed Zhou. A smile always graced his round face, and his side-parted hair was meticulously combed. Whenever he went out on business, Mr. Zhou wore a dark brown silk long gown woven with circular "longevity" characters, topped with a high-quality riding jacket. In the small pocket on the upper left of his chest was a gold pocket watch, attached to a gold chain with the other end tightly fastened to a button on his chest. He wore a pair of rimless, gold-edged glasses, stepped into a pair of shiny brownish-black leather shoes, and held a walking stick (cane) in his hand. He walked with an imposing air and a very gentlemanly demeanor.

The Zhou Mansion was luxurious and grand, and the family was wealthy. Mr. Zhou had four sons and two daughters, and his in-laws were all from wealthy and noble families. When Mr. Zhou learned that Second Uncle had a military background, interacted with foreigners, and was single, he wanted to betroth his second daughter to him.

This second daughter was twenty-three years old. Five years earlier, she had been betrothed to the son of a wealthy landlord surnamed Zhao. When she reached the age of twenty and was preparing to marry, an unexpected disaster suddenly struck—the Zhao family's son fell ill and died suddenly while transporting goods. The second daughter stayed at home for three long years, and no one dared to propose marriage, claiming that she was cursed with bad luck and fearing that marrying her would bring misfortune. Seeing that his daughter's marriage was difficult to arrange, Mr. Zhou was extremely anxious.

During a ceramic chamber of commerce meeting, Mr. Zhou made a point of interacting warmly with Second Uncle and invited him to be a guest at his home. After getting to know him thoroughly, Mr. Zhou felt very satisfied and determined to secure this son-in-law. From then on, with fine wine, good food, and warm hospitality, Second Uncle became a regular honored guest at his house.

The Zhou family continuously looked after Second Uncle in his daily life and even provided a significant amount of financial assistance to help him set up his own porcelain business, purchasing a building and a home for him. Second Uncle did not believe in superstition or fear ghosts; he couldn't care less about so-called bad luck or curses. How could anyone resist such good fortune? Without even seeking the advice of his mother and eldest brother, he was set to marry the Zhou family's second daughter.

Mr. Zhou felt that his second daughter had suffered too much injustice and worried that she might not receive respect after marriage, so he made the wedding exceptionally grand and luxurious. I remember his wedding chamber was in a villa located in a large alley, with a unique, East-meets-West decor style. It had a spacious living room with a long, wide pavilion-style alcove at the upper end. In front of the alcove stood a mahogany Eight Immortals table, flanked by two carved chairs, with matching chairs and tea tables on both sides. Hanging on the two walls were Chinese landscape paintings and antique scrolls.

Passing through the main living room, at the back was their private small sitting room, furnished with leather sofas. In front of the sofas was a small square table adorned with exquisite ornaments. Near the windowsill stood an antique gramophone, which continuously played melodious and beautiful music. On the walls hung Western landscape paintings and abstract oil paintings, embellishing the entire atmosphere with warmth and elegance.

On the day of the wedding, a tall, colorful ceremonial archway appeared directly in front of the large alley. Bright red and purple flowers, along with colorful ribbons and balloons, danced in the wind, welcoming the arrival of the bride.

The bride sat in a four-man-carried bridal sedan chair draped with bright red silk curtains. In front, a Western drum-and-bugle band played music, marching forward slowly with steady, rhythmic steps. Closely following was the "Ten-Mile Red Dowry," with pairs of people carrying heavy dowry chests, walking slowly one after another. The bride's jewelry boxes contained pearl necklaces, jade pendants, and emerald rings... Firecrackers shook the heavens, and firecrackers danced all over the streets. The wedding banquet lasted for three full days and nights, bursting with extraordinary liveliness.

After the Liberation, Second Uncle faced major setbacks politically and in business. Because of his resilient spirit and mental preparedness, he always said optimistically, "I am a veteran athlete of political movements; whenever a movement comes, I am never left out." He added, "After a heavy rain, some see a rainbow, while others see a ground full of mud. However, my heart is always filled with hope."

With the porcelain business suspended, Second Uncle's family moved to Shanghai. Second Uncle was hired by a middle school and became an English teacher. He always believed that God watched over him; the priest had taught him English, and when times were toughest, English actually turned out to be of great use.

With the reform and opening up, before Second Uncle retired, an elderly gentleman suddenly came one day and knocked on the neighbor's door. The door remained closed for a long time. Seeing this, Second Uncle invited the old gentleman into his home to sit for a while and asked about his purpose. It turned out that the gentleman was a senior alumnus of the Whampoa Military Academy, who had traveled from the United States back to Shanghai specifically to find a very close former classmate and see what help he could offer him.

Unfortunately, the classmate next door had passed away many years ago. With red-rimmed eyes, the old gentleman said, "I really didn't expect that we would never meet again."

When the old gentleman learned that Second Uncle was also a Whampoa Military Academy graduate from a certain class, he recalled a junior classmate named Peng Changming. Recalling their school days, they had endless stories to tell and share... The old gentleman asked sincerely, "Is there anything you need help with?" Shortly afterward, Second Uncle took his wife and boarded a China Eastern Airlines flight to the United States. The old gentleman arranged for their food, clothing, housing, and transportation, allowing them to live a life free of worries.
