# 二叔(下)

**Date:** 2025-06-01 02:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷

  

图/123RF

一九四九年，父亲把二叔安排到江西景德镇去帮忙办货，打理瓷器生意。二叔本性不是做生意的料，他好动、坐不住，喜欢体育运动，参加各种球模拟赛，各种球艺都很精通。他在篮球比赛中结交了一位外国神父，两人非常投缘，成为好朋友。在交往中，神父教二叔英文，他们用英文交流会话，二叔的英文水平突飞猛进，一口流利的英语让人羡慕，在神父的传教下，还成为了一名虔诚的信徒。

景德镇陶瓷商会主席是一名六十岁开外姓周的老先生，他圆圆的脸庞上总挂着笑容，三七开缝的头发，梳理得一丝不苟。每逢外出办事，周老先生身穿一件深褐色丝绸长衫，上面织有圆形寿字图案，外面套上优质面料的马褂，胸前左上方的小口袋里装上金色怀表，表上牵系着一条金链子，另一端紧紧扣住胸前的钮扣上。他戴了一副无框架的金水边眼镜，脚下蹬上一双棕黑色珵亮的皮鞋，手上拿了一根文明棍（拐杖），走起路来很气派，挺有绅士之风度。

周公馆豪华、气派，家庭富裕；周老先生有四儿两女，亲家都是豪门贵族。当周先生知道二叔是军人出身，又跟外国人有交往，并是单身，便想把他的二姑娘许配给二叔。

这位二姑娘芳龄二十三，五年之前，已订婚许配给一户赵姓财主家的儿子。姑娘到了二十岁那年准备出嫁，却突然遭遇不测风云，横祸降临，赵家的儿子在运送货物途中暴病身亡。二姑娘在家待了三年之久，没有人敢来提亲，说是这姑娘霉气很重，都害怕与她和合。眼看女儿的终身大事难以求成，这也急坏了周老先生。

在开陶瓷商会的时候，周先生特意和二叔亲切交互，并邀请二叔去他家作客，经过全方面的了解，周先生感到很满意，下定决心要套牢这位女婿。从此，好酒、好菜、好招待，二叔便成了他家的座上客。

周家不断在生活上照料二叔，还拿出不少资金援助，让他自立门户做瓷器生意，为他买楼置房。二叔不信邪、不怕鬼，什么晦气、霉气他全不在乎，这样的好运怎能抵挡得了？他也不征求母亲和大哥的意见，就要和周家的二姑娘结婚了。

周先生觉得二姑娘太受委屈，怕她婚后得不到尊重，因此把婚礼搞得特别气派、豪华。记得他的婚房是在一条大巷子里的别墅，布置风格奇特，中西结合，有宽敞的大客厅，客厅上方有一个宽长的条亭，条亭的前面有个红木八仙桌，两旁两把雕花椅子，左右两边也有椅子和茶几搭配；两面墙上悬挂着中国山水画和古色古香的条幅。

穿过大客厅，后面是自家的小客厅，放的都是皮沙发，沙发前面有小方台，配着精致的摆饰。窗台旁有个古老的留声机，不断传出悠悠动听的音乐；墙上挂的是西洋风景画和抽象派的油画，把整个气氛点缀得温馨、优雅。

迎亲那天，大巷子正前方出现了一座高大的彩牌楼，上面大红大紫艳丽的鲜花和各种各色的彩带、气球随风起舞，迎接着新娘到来。

新娘子坐在披着大红丝幛四人擡的花轿内，前面有洋鼓洋号的乐队奏曲，吹吹打打整齐的步伐慢慢前进着。后面紧跟着「十里红妆」，由两人一组，一前一后擡着沉甸甸的嫁妆缓步前行。随同新娘子的珠宝首饰盒里，装有珍珠项链、玉石配带和祖母绿戒指……。炮竹震天响，鞭炮满街舞，婚宴足足举办了三天三夜，热闹非凡。

解放后，二叔在政治上、生意上受到很大冲击，由于他有一股刚毅之气，也有心理准备，他总乐观地说：「我是个老运动员，每次运动来了，都少不了我。」又补充道：「一场大雨过后，有人看到的是一片彩虹，有人看到一地污泥。但是，我心中永远装满了希望。」

瓷器生意停摆，二叔一家来到上海，二叔被应聘到一所中学，成为一名英语教师。二叔一直认为上帝眷顾他，神父教他学会了英文，到了最困难的时候，英语竟派上了大用场。

改革开放了，在二叔[**退休**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/退休)之前，一天忽然来了一位老先生敲隔壁邻居的大门，大门久久未开，二叔见状，请老先生进他屋里稍坐，问清楚老先生的来意。原来，老先生是黄埔军校高年级的学生，他特地从美国回上海，寻找他很要好的老同学，看看能帮助他做点什么。

遗憾的是，邻居老同学在好多年前就过世了。老先生红着眼圈说：「真没想到，从此就见不到面了。」

当老先生知道二叔也是黄埔军校的同学，而且是某届某班的学生，他一时想起了低年级是有一个叫彭长鸣的人。他俩回忆当年学校的场景，有说不完的话，讲不尽的故事……。老先生真挚地问：「你有什么需要帮忙的？」不久，二叔携带着婶娘乘上了[**东方航空**](https://www.worldjournal.com/search/tagging/8877/东方航空)飞机，来到了美国，老先生为他们安排了衣食住行，过上后顾无忧的生活。

## English Translation
# Second Uncle (Part II)

**Date:** June 1, 2025, 02:00 AM ET  
**Author:** Peng Lihe  

Image/123RF  

In 1949, my father arranged for my Second Uncle to go to Jingdezhen, Jiangxi, to help source goods and manage the family porcelain business. By nature, Second Uncle was not cut out for business. He was active, restless, and loved sports; he participated in various exhibition ball games and was highly proficient in all kinds of ball sports. During a basketball match, he befriended a foreign priest. The two hit it off immediately and became close friends. As they spent time together, the priest taught Second Uncle English, and they conversed only in English. Consequently, Second Uncle's English improved by leaps and bounds, and his fluent speech became the envy of others. Under the priest's missionary guidance, he also became a devout believer.

The president of the Jingdezhen Ceramic Chamber of Commerce was an old gentleman in his sixties named Mr. Zhou. He always wore a smile on his round face, and his hair, parted neatly in a three-to-seven ratio, was meticulously combed. Whenever he went out on business, Mr. Zhou wore a dark brown silk long gown woven with circular longevity patterns, layered with a high-quality vest. In the small upper-left pocket of his vest, he kept a gold pocket watch attached to a gold chain that was securely buckled to his chest button. He wore a pair of rimless, gold-rimmed glasses, polished brownish-black leather shoes, and carried a gentleman’s walking cane. He walked with immense poise and carried himself with a highly gentlemanly demeanor.

The Zhou Mansion was luxurious and grand, and the family was exceedingly wealthy. Mr. Zhou had four sons and two daughters, and all of his in-laws were from aristocratic and wealthy families. When Mr. Zhou learned that Second Uncle had a military background, associated with foreigners, and was still single, he wanted to betroth his second daughter to him.

This second daughter was twenty-three years old. Five years prior, she had been betrothed to the son of a wealthy landlord named Zhao. When the girl turned twenty and was preparing to marry, an unexpected tragedy struck: the Zhao family's son died suddenly of a swift illness while transporting goods. The second daughter remained at home for three long years, and no one dared to ask for her hand in marriage, claiming she was plagued by bad luck and fearing the misfortune she might bring. Seeing his daughter's marriage prospects look increasingly bleak, Mr. Zhou was also extremely anxious.

During Ceramic Chamber of Commerce meetings, Mr. Zhou made a point of interacting warmly with Second Uncle and invited him to his home as a guest. After getting to know him thoroughly, Mr. Zhou was very pleased and became determined to secure him as his son-in-law. From then on, with fine wine, exquisite food, and warm hospitality, Second Uncle became a regular honored guest at their home.

The Zhou family not only constantly looked after Second Uncle's daily life, but also provided substantial financial aid to help him start his own porcelain business and purchase property. Second Uncle did not believe in superstition or ghosts; he cared nothing for talk of curses or bad luck. How could anyone resist such good fortune? Without consulting his mother or eldest brother, he decided to marry the Zhou family's second daughter.

Mr. Zhou felt his second daughter had already suffered too much grievance and worried she might not receive proper respect after marriage, so he made the wedding exceptionally grand and luxurious. I remember their wedding home was a villa nestled in a large alley. The interior style was unique, blending Chinese and Western elements. It featured a spacious living room with a wide, long pavilion-like area above it. In front of the pavilion area sat a mahogany Eight Immortals table, flanked by two carved chairs, with matching chairs and tea tables on either side. Chinese landscape paintings and antique hanging scrolls adorned both walls.

Passing through the grand living room led to their private small living room, which was furnished with leather sofas. In front of the sofas stood small square tables adorned with delicate ornaments. Next to the window sill was an antique gramophone that continuously played melodious music. Western landscape paintings and abstract oil paintings hung on the walls, making the entire atmosphere warm, cozy, and elegant.

On the day of the wedding, a tall, colorful ceremonial archway was erected at the entrance of the alley. It was decorated with vibrant red and purple flowers, colorful ribbons, and balloons dancing in the wind to welcome the bride.

The bride rode inside a four-person sedan chair draped in bright red silk, preceded by a band playing foreign drums and brass horns, marching forward with orderly steps. Following closely behind was the "ten-mile bridal dowry," carried by pairs of people bearing heavy dowry chests one after another. Inside the bride's jewelry boxes were pearl necklaces, jade pendants, and emerald rings. Firecrackers shook the skies, sparks danced across the streets, and the wedding feast lasted for three days and three nights in an incredibly lively celebration.

After the founding of the People's Republic of China, Second Uncle suffered major setbacks both politically and in business. However, possessing a resilient spirit and being mentally prepared, he would always say optimistically: "I am a veteran 'athlete'; whenever a political campaign comes around, I am always in the thick of it." He would add: "After a heavy rain, some see a rainbow, while others see nothing but mud. But my heart is always filled with hope."

As the porcelain business ground to a halt, Second Uncle's family moved to Shanghai, where he was hired by a middle school to work as an English teacher. Second Uncle always believed that God was watching over him; the English taught to him by the priest had ultimately saved him in his time of greatest need.

With the launch of the Reform and Opening-up, shortly before Second Uncle retired, an elderly gentleman suddenly came knocking on his neighbor's door. The door remained closed for a long time. Seeing this, Second Uncle invited the old gentleman into his home to rest and asked about his business. It turned out the old gentleman was a senior alumnus of the Whampoa Military Academy who had returned to Shanghai from the United States specifically to find his close classmate and see if he could offer any help.

Regrettably, the neighbor classmate had passed away many years before. With tearful eyes, the old gentleman remarked, "I never imagined we would never meet again."

When the old gentleman discovered that Second Uncle was also a Whampoa alumnus—specifically from a certain class—he suddenly recalled a junior student named Peng Changming. As the two reminisced about their school days, they found they had endless things to talk about and stories to share. The old gentleman asked sincerely, "Is there anything you need help with?" Not long after, Second Uncle and his wife boarded an Eastern Airlines flight to the United States. The old gentleman arranged for their housing, food, and daily needs, allowing them to spend the rest of their lives free from worries.
