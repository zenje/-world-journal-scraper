# 一百枚大银圆(下)

**Date:** 2026-05-03 02:00 ET
**Author:** 彭丽荷

第二天的清辰，大公鸡喔喔啼的声音把我弄醒了，我一骨碌爬起来，就参观了姑婆家的住舍：有一间大堂屋，另一间就是我们睡觉的房间，这房间不是很宽，但特别长，靠顶端墙处放着一张大床，一个木制的破旧柜子上面的油漆也都脱落，呈现出东一块、西一块的疤痕。房间的另一端有一个很大的灶炉、水缸、锅碗瓢盆等，还有凌乱的一大堆柴火，我感到很奇怪，怎么厨房和房间是在同一个屋子里的？

再看看堂屋，左侧堆满了玉米棒子、稻谷和小麦，还有一些农具；右侧有两条长板凳上架着一个高高隆起、用芦苇盖没的土包子堆，最上面有一层大草席将这个土包子覆盖得严严实实。我好奇地问祖母：「那是什么东西？」祖母却严肃回答：「小孩不要问那么多。」愈是叫不要问，我愈是想知道。

我吓了一大跳，原本胆子小的我，缩到了一个角落里，不敢睁开眼睛，原来那是姑婆百年之后用的一口棺材，用黑漆油得亮崭崭的，棺材盖有些隆起，棺材一头大一头小，怪吓人的。从此，我不愿意去那个堂屋。

直到有一天，姑婆从棺材里拿出了一个布袋子，里面装的全是银圆，我也不识货，不知这银圆是用来干嘛的，只觉得每个银圆闪耀着亮珵珵的光芒，上面有个胖呼呼、额头鼓鼓的人像，看上去很讨人喜欢。

这是姑婆一辈子的积蓄。姑婆说她无儿无女，自己上了年纪，要把这些银圆全部都让祖母带回上海，让我买好多东西吃。我真没想到，棺材里会有那么多钱。姑婆却说：「看见了棺材，棺材、棺材，就会发财。」我半信半疑，慢慢地胆子大起来了，解除了思想上的包袱，也不害怕了。

一天，姑婆邀请我到镇上去看戏，那个戏台是搭建在四周有砖墙的一个院落之中，买了戏票就可以进去落座喝茶看戏。当姑婆知道戏票大人和小孩都要五毛钱一张，算一算三人要一块五毛钱，这笔钱够姑婆半个月的生活费，她皱了皱眉对我说：「太贵了，我有办法不花钱，让妳能看到戏。」

姑婆借来一条长梯子，长梯子的一头靠在院落墙壁上，只要爬到梯子顶端，就可以看到院落中央的戏台。姑婆叫我往梯子上爬，她却在后面跟着我，叫我放大胆子，她会保护我的，祖母站在地面上，扶稳梯子，叫我看戏。

锣鼓声此起彼伏不断地敲响着，大约十分钟过去了，戏台上没有一个人出现。又过去了十来分钟，从戏台左右两侧出来两个连续翻跟头、身穿黑色、腰间绑著白色带子的武功演员，他们不断地在舞台上翻来翻去，翻个不完。我趴在梯子上，神经一直紧绷着，等待了很久也不见有新花样，最后累了，疲劳地打着哈欠说不想看了。这一幕场景类似鲁迅先生笔下的「社戏」一文。

在姑婆家要住一个多月，姑婆偶尔带我去镇上买些米花糖、葵瓜子、山芋干等农村的土特产让我吃，还为我们杀鸡宰鸭、炖排骨莲藕汤，招待贵客的礼节都用上了。平时姑婆省吃俭用，一个咸鸭蛋都要吃好几顿，她对待我们算得上十分大方。

姑婆还为我找来几个村里的小朋友跟我玩耍。某日，姑婆和祖母一起去亲戚家办点事，我不愿意跟着她们去，有几个小朋友来家里陪我玩。我也不知青红皂白、天高地厚，把那袋装有银圆的布袋打开，银圆散落满地，大家一起欢快地玩。正玩得高兴时，祖母和姑婆回到家，见到如此景象，姑婆脸色一下子严肃起来，甚至大声吼叫：「你们这些小鬼，这是拿来玩的吗？把你们的爹妈叫来！」并拿起扫帚朝那几个小朋友打去，孩子们吓得赶紧窜逃。

我不知所措，哇哇地大哭起来。祖母、姑婆立即把银圆清点收回布袋，一面数着数，一面往布袋中放，点到最后只有九十七枚，缺少三枚。姑婆把里里外外都找遍了，没有看见，她气喘吁吁，额头上冒出了汗珠，非常生气的样子，碍着祖母的面子，不好发作。我垂着头，不敢看她一眼。

到了晚上，只见那个推独轮车的中年男子揪着他女儿的耳朵走进姑婆家，一进门就把女儿压着地上跪着，欠着身子向姑婆连连道歉说：「大姑奶奶，我家的孩子不懂事，拿了妳家三枚银圆放在口袋里，被我发觉后，狠狠地抽她一顿，请您老原谅她。」姑婆摆了摆手，看见三枚银圆失而复得，气消了一半。

祖母趁机打个圆场说：「没事、没事，孩子不是故意的，只是觉得这玩意儿挺新鲜，随手拿了三枚放在口袋里。」我害怕地躲在门后，从门缝里见到那女孩的眼泪直往地面上滴，我一时冲动大叫起来，泪水也布满了整个面额。

过了几天，仍然是那个中年男子推着独轮车把我们送到码头边，我拿下了背在肩上的红色皮包包，里面还有不少糖豆豆，递到中年男子的手中说：「把这个送给你女儿。」一百枚银圆在行李箱中，随同我们一起回到上海。

## English Translation
One Hundred Large Silver Dollars (Part II)

On the morning of the second day, I was woken up by the crowing of a rooster. Rolling out of bed, I took a tour of my great-aunt's house. There was a main hall, and another room where we slept. This bedroom was not very wide but exceptionally long. Near the far wall stood a large bed, next to an old, worn wooden cabinet whose peeling paint left ugly scars here and there. At the other end of the room was a large stove, a water vat, pots, pans, and a messy pile of firewood. I found it very strange—why were the kitchen and the bedroom in the same room?

Looking at the main hall again, the left side was piled high with corn cobs, paddy rice, wheat, and some farming tools. On the right side, two long wooden benches supported a highly raised, reed-covered earthen mound, with a large straw mat tightly covering the very top of it. Curious, I asked my grandmother, "What is that?" My grandmother replied sternly, "Children shouldn't ask so many questions." The more I was told not to ask, the more I wanted to know.

I got a big fright. Being naturally timid, I shrank into a corner, not daring to open my eyes. It turned out to be the coffin my great-aunt would use after she passed away. It was painted with shiny black lacquer, with a slightly raised lid. It was larger at one end and smaller at the other, which was quite terrifying. From then on, I refused to go into that main hall.

Until one day, my great-aunt pulled a cloth bag from the coffin. It was filled with silver dollars. I didn't know their value back then or what they were used for; I just thought each silver dollar shone with a bright, gleaming luster. They had a chubby, prominent-foreheaded portrait on them, which looked very endearing.

These were my great-aunt’s life savings. She said she had no children and was getting old, so she wanted my grandmother to take all these silver dollars back to Shanghai to buy me lots of delicious things to eat. I never imagined there would be so much money inside a coffin. But my great-aunt said, "When you see a coffin, it means you will strike it rich." Half-believing her, my courage gradually grew, I let go of my mental burden, and I was no longer afraid.

One day, my great-aunt invited me to the town to watch a play. The stage was set up inside a courtyard surrounded by brick walls. Once you bought a ticket, you could go inside, take a seat, drink tea, and watch the play. When my great-aunt found out that tickets were fifty cents each for both adults and children, she calculated that it would cost one dollar and fifty cents for the three of us. This amount was enough to cover her living expenses for half a month. Frowning, she said to me, "It's too expensive. I have a way for you to see the play without spending any money."

My great-aunt borrowed a long ladder and leaned one end of it against the courtyard wall. As long as I climbed to the top of the ladder, I could see the stage in the center of the courtyard. She told me to climb up while she followed behind, telling me to be brave and promising to protect me. My grandmother stood on the ground, holding the ladder steady, and told me to watch the play.

The sound of gongs and drums clashed continuously. About ten minutes passed, but not a single person appeared on the stage. Another ten minutes or so went by, and from the left and right sides of the stage came two martial arts actors dressed in black with white sashes tied around their waists, doing continuous somersaults. They kept flipping back and forth across the stage without end. Clinging to the ladder, my nerves were constantly tense. I waited for a long time but saw no new tricks. Finally, exhausted and yawning, I said I didn't want to watch anymore. This scene was quite similar to the one in Mr. Lu Xun's story "Village Opera."

During our stay of over a month at my great-aunt's house, she would occasionally take me to town to buy local rural specialties like puffed rice candy, sunflower seeds, and dried sweet potatoes for me to eat. She even slaughtered chickens and ducks and stewed pork rib and lotus root soup for us, using all the etiquette reserved for honored guests. Usually, she lived very frugally, making a single salted duck egg last for several meals, but she was incredibly generous to us.

My great-aunt also found a few children from the village to play with me. One day, when my great-aunt and grandmother went to a relative's house to run some errands, I didn't want to go with them, so a few children came over to keep me company. Oblivious to the consequences, I opened the cloth bag containing the silver dollars, letting them scatter all over the floor, and we all played happily together. Just as we were having a great time, my grandmother and great-aunt returned home. Seeing this, my great-aunt's face instantly grew stern, and she even shouted loudly, "You little rascals, is this something to play with? Go call your parents!" She grabbed a broom and swung it at the children, who fled in terror.

At a loss of what to do, I burst into loud tears. My grandmother and great-aunt immediately began counting and putting the silver dollars back into the bag, counting them aloud as they put them in. In the end, there were only ninety-seven, three short. My great-aunt searched inside and out but couldn't find them. Gasping for breath, with beads of sweat on her forehead, she looked extremely angry, though out of respect for my grandmother, she restrained her temper. I hung my head, not daring to look at her.

In the evening, the middle-aged man who pushed the wheelbarrow came in, pulling his daughter by the ear. As soon as he entered, he forced his daughter to kneel on the ground and bowed slightly to apologize to my great-aunt repeatedly, saying, "Great-Auntie, my child is young and foolish. She took three silver dollars and put them in her pocket. After I found out, I gave her a good whipping. Please forgive her." My great-aunt waved her hand. Seeing the three silver dollars returned, her anger subsided by half.

My grandmother seized the opportunity to smooth things over, saying, "It's fine, it's fine. The child didn't do it on purpose; she just found them novel and slipped three of them into her pocket." I hid fearfully behind the door. Peering through the crack, I saw the girl's tears dripping onto the floor. On an impulse, I cried out, my own face covered in tears.

A few days later, the same middle-aged man pushed his wheelbarrow to take us to the pier. I took off the red leather bag on my shoulder, which still had quite a few candy drops inside, handed it to him, and said, "Give this to your daughter." The one hundred silver dollars, packed inside our suitcase, traveled back to Shanghai with us.
